# Case Study: Wave Utilities — Automated Debt Enforcement Against a Disabled Customer

**Case Reference:** Wave / iC&R / LJ Barbers — Accounts 31694322001 & 31694322002
**Date Resolved:** 30 March 2026
**Sector:** Water Retail (Non-Household)
**Outcome:** Both accounts cleared to £0.00. £795.14 in fees and interest removed. All enforcement ceased. Reasonable adjustments recorded.

---

## Summary

Wave Utilities referred two water accounts to debt collection agent iC&R through an automated process that did not involve any individual human review. The automated system pursued £679.38 from a disabled, self-employed customer who had already made three payments totalling £875 and whose actual combined balance was approximately £35. When a single human being individually reviewed the accounts, every issue was resolved in one exchange — accounts cleared, £795.14 in fees removed, enforcement withdrawn, and reasonable adjustments pinned to both accounts.

---

## Background

Lewis James Burgess operates LJ Barbers at 26A Duke Street, Darlington, DL3 7AQ. He has bilateral sensorineural hearing loss and has asserted email-only communication as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.

Wave Utilities (NWG Business Limited) held two accounts for the same premises: account 31694322001 (26A Duke Street) and account 31694322002 (First Floor, 26A Duke Street), each with a separate meter. Both accounts were opened on 15 April 2016 and migrated automatically from Northumbrian Water at the retail market opening on 1 April 2017. No signed contract exists — the complainant is on a deemed contract under Ofwat's Retail Exit Code.

iC&R, an accredited entity acting strictly as a data processor on behalf of Wave, was instructed to pursue the debt. iC&R attended the complainant's business premises and threatened disconnection of water supply. Kenny Allport of Wave Credit & Collections authored a letter dated 3 March 2026 demanding £518.88, applying £6.24 late payment interest under the Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998, threatening disconnection within 7 days, and threatening litigation.

At the time of enforcement, the complainant had already made three payments totalling £875. The actual combined balance across both accounts was approximately £35.

---

## The SOVEREIGN/NULL Question

The complainant put a single question to Wave:

> "Confirmation of whether any individual at iC&R verified the current account balance before attending my premises or threatening disconnection."

This is the Burgess Principle test applied directly: was a human mind with proper authority individually applied to the specific facts of this specific case before the institution acted against the individual?

---

## The Institution's Answer

Erica, Senior Customer Service, Complex Queries Department at Wave, responded at point 2(d):

> "This is an automatic process in the system where accounts get referrals after they hit a certain amount of outstanding debt. However, before referring the accounts to the accredited entities (ICR) they go through a series of checks. The decisions are based on the Wave T&C's, Wave policies and procedures and all applicable laws/regulations related to the water industry, debt collection and Accredited Entities."

**Finding: NULL.**

No individual human being reviewed the complainant's account before enforcement was instructed. The referral was automated. The system processed the account based on a debt threshold. Nobody checked whether the balance reflected payments already received. Nobody checked whether the complainant was disabled. Nobody checked whether enforcement at business premises was proportionate.

## The Resolution

When Erica individually reviewed both accounts, the following actions were taken in a single exchange:

1. **Both accounts confirmed at £0.00.** £70.79 credit from account 001 was transferred to account 002. The remaining £34.80 was cleared as a goodwill gesture.
2. **Late payment fees removed:** £379.00 (account 001) + £364.50 (account 002) = **£743.50**.
3. **Late payment interest removed:** £17.35 (account 001) + £34.29 (account 002) = **£51.64**.
4. **Total fees and interest removed: £795.14.**
5. **iC&R pulled back** — all enforcement ceased.
6. **Confirmed the iC&R referral was an automatic process** with no individual review.
7. **Confirmed no signed contract exists** — the complainant is on a deemed contract under Ofwat's Retail Exit Code.
8. **Email-only reasonable adjustment notes pinned** to both accounts.
9. **Email invoicing activated.**
10. **Confirmed any future site visits** would be arranged in advance by email.

## Doctrinal Analysis

This case demonstrates the complete Burgess Principle cycle from NULL to SOVEREIGN.

**NULL identified.** Enforcement was automated. No human reviewed the account before iC&R was dispatched. The system processed a debt threshold and generated a referral without checking whether payments had been received, whether the balance was current, or whether the account holder was disabled.

**Consequence of NULL.** iC&R pursued £679.38 from a customer who owed approximately £35. Enforcement agents attended a disabled person's business premises. Disconnection of water supply was threatened. Late payment interest was applied to a balance that did not reflect payments already allocated. £795.14 in fees and interest was charged on accounts where the actual combined shortfall was £35.

**SOVEREIGN applied.** When a human being (Erica) individually reviewed the accounts, every issue resolved in a single exchange. The accounts were cleared. The fees were removed. The enforcement was stood down. The reasonable adjustment was recorded. The question the automated system could not answer — *"is this right for this specific person?"* — was answered in minutes by a person who actually looked.

**The contrast.** The automated system generated months of enforcement, multiple premises attendances, disconnection threats, and £795.14 in fees. One human review resolved everything. The cost of the absent human was measured in distress, business disruption, and nearly £800 in unjustified charges. The cost of the present human was one email exchange.

## Lesson

Any citizen facing automated enforcement should ask one question: did a human being individually review my account before this action was taken? If the answer is no — if the process was triggered by a system threshold, a bulk referral, or an automated rule — then the enforcement was initiated without anyone checking whether it was right for the specific person in the specific case. This case proves that the question works. The institution confirmed the referral was automatic. When a human finally looked, every charge, every threat, and every attendance was reversed in a single exchange. The principle does not require legal training, representation, or confrontation. It requires one question, asked clearly, answered in writing, and documented. The automated system could not distinguish between a customer who owed £679 and a customer who owed £35. A human could. That is the Burgess Principle.
