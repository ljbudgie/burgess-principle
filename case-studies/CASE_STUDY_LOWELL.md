## Summary

Getting letters about an old purchased debt can feel exhausting. It can feel even harder when the debt has moved from the original company to a debt purchaser and no one will clearly explain who checked the facts. Many people feel overwhelmed when this happens. You are not alone.

In this case, Lowell Financial Ltd pursued a debt bought from **npower** relating to a gas supply period from **29 June 2018 to 30 September 2019**. The challenge asked a simple question: before collection started, did any named human being with proper authority actually check whether the debt was validly attributed, whether the period was right, whether the balance was properly supported, whether the debt was already statute-barred, and whether reasonable adjustments were needed?

Lowell never answered that question directly. Instead, after about two months of correspondence, it issued a final response on **13 April 2026** saying the complaint was **not upheld** while also confirming that **no further recovery action** would be taken and the **account would be closed**. That matters because it shows something important and hopeful: a structured challenge can stop the machinery even when the institution will not formally concede the point.

- **Relevant account, document, or reference:** Lowell reference 382564532; original npower account 212216345; complaint reference 614420.
- **Disability / adjustment context:** Bilateral sensorineural hearing loss; email-only communication asserted under sections 20 and 29 of the Equality Act 2010.
- **What the institution did:** Lowell pursued a purchased npower debt for **£563.14** linked to a previous supply period from **29 June 2018 to 30 September 2019**.
- **Why the case matters:** It shows how a purchased-debt pipeline can keep moving until a person asks for proof of human review, limitation, accessibility compliance, and data-rights compliance all at once.

The challenge disputed liability for the full period, questioned whether the balance had been individually verified, raised the statute-barred aspect, and required email-only communication because of hearing loss. Lowell then directed the DSAR process toward an online portal rather than simply dealing with the request by email. In an accessibility context, that matters. A channel redirection can become a barrier when a person has already explained what communication method they need.

This case sits in the Burgess Principle evidence base because Lowell ultimately chose abandonment over explanation. The institution did not uphold the complaint. It also did not continue recovery. It simply closed the account once the record became too specific to ignore.

## The Key SOVEREIGN / NULL Question

> "Before any collection activity was initiated against me, did a human mind with proper authority individually review my specific circumstances — whether the debt was validly attributed to me, whether the period was correct, whether the original creditor's records supported the full balance, whether the debt was already statute-barred, whether I have a disability, whether reasonable adjustments were required — and individually authorise the pursuit of this debt?"

This is the question that matters most. In calm, practical terms, it asks whether a real person checked the actual facts before the recovery process started. If no named person reviewed those facts before Lowell began collecting, the operative path was **NULL**.

## If This Is Happening To You

If a debt purchaser is writing to you about an old debt, try to slow the process down. You do not need to solve everything at once. Take it one step at a time.

Many purchased-debt cases follow a bulk workflow. That means the letters can feel very certain even when the underlying record is thin. A calm written challenge can make a big difference.

### Gentle first steps

1. **Keep the issue factual.** Ask whether anyone actually checked that the debt is yours, that the amount is right, and that the period being claimed is correct.
2. **Check limitation early.** If the debt may be statute-barred, say so clearly. Do not ignore the timeline.
3. **Do not acknowledge the debt by accident.** Avoid making a payment or using wording that accepts liability while you are still checking the facts.
4. **Ask for adjustments straight away if you need them.** If phone calls are inaccessible, say you need email-only communication or another reasonable adjustment.
5. **Use separate tracks if it helps.** Keep liability, limitation, reasonable adjustments, and data rights as distinct strands. That makes the record easier to follow later.

You are not alone if this feels draining. The goal is not to win an argument in one letter. The goal is to create a clear record that the institution cannot easily automate past.

## Doctrinal Notes

Lowell establishes the third completed resolution pattern in the case-studies collection.

- [Wave Utilities](CASE_STUDY_WAVE.md) shows **NULL corrected by named human review**.
- [TV Licensing](CASE_STUDY_TVL.md) shows **NULL exposed because the original enforcement premise was false**.
- Lowell shows **NULL abandoned without concession**.

That third pattern matters. It shows that an institution may refuse to admit the point and still retreat completely once a challenge becomes specific enough to require real human accountability. For stressed readers, that is an important practical lesson. Full concession is not the only meaningful outcome. Sometimes the machinery stops because it cannot comfortably survive scrutiny.
