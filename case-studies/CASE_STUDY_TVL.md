# Case Study: TV Licensing (BBC / Capita) — Automated Enforcement Letters Sent Without Checking Whether a Licence Was Needed

- **Case Reference:** POLSAR1321
- **Sector:** Broadcasting Enforcement
- **Current Status:** RESOLVED
- **Date Opened:** Before April 2026
- **Date Resolved:** April 2026
- **Current Finding:** NULL
- **Response Classification:** AMBIGUOUS
- **Procedural Posture:** Closed after the address was registered as "No Licence Needed" and removed from the automated correspondence cycle.
- **Last Updated:** 13 April 2026
- **Outcome:** Threatening letters ceased. Address status was registered as "No Licence Needed" under reference 4315773702, valid until the end of March 2027.

---

## Summary

TV Licensing, operated by Capita on behalf of the BBC, sent escalating enforcement letters to an address without any identified human being first checking whether the occupier owned a television, watched live broadcasts, or used BBC iPlayer. The correspondence escalated to implied prosecution and a £1,000 fine on the basis of an absent licence record alone. When the address was registered as "No Licence Needed," the letters stopped. This is a resolved NULL case in a different pattern from Wave: not debt NULL to £0.00 after human review, but enforcement NULL to correspondence ceased because the premise was false from the start.

## Background

- **Complainant / applicant:** Lewis James Burgess.
- **Relevant account, document, or reference:** SAR reference POLSAR1321; "No Licence Needed" registration reference 4315773702.
- **Disability / adjustment context:** Bilateral sensorineural hearing loss; email-only communication asserted under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.
- **What the institution did:** TV Licensing sent physical letters in escalating enforcement language without first checking whether the licence requirement applied to the specific address or whether reasonable adjustments were required.
- **Why the case matters:** It shows a pure enforcement-presumption model. Unlike a debt case where a balance can later be corrected, this case began from a premise that had never been individually verified at all.

The complainant's position was that no TV licence was required. Section 363 of the Communications Act 2003 criminalises unlicensed installation or use of television-receiving equipment for live broadcast reception; the absence of a licence on a database is not itself proof that those facts exist. The challenge also asserted data-rights requests under UK GDPR Articles 15, 21, and 22. BBC iPlayer use was part of the factual question asked, but the core point remained the same: TV Licensing had not individually checked whether the legal preconditions for enforcement messaging were present before acting.

---

## The SOVEREIGN / NULL Question

> "Before any letter was sent to my address, did a human mind with proper authority individually review my specific circumstances — whether I own a television, whether I watch live broadcasts, whether I use BBC iPlayer, whether I have a disability, whether reasonable adjustments are required — and individually authorise the correspondence?"

This question isolates the decisive step. If no named person checked whether the licence regime applied to this address before enforcement letters were issued, the operative path was NULL.
