## Core Principle – The Binary Test

Any instrument (state, corporate, algorithmic, or technological) that exercises authority over a citizen must satisfy one question:

**Was a human judicial mind applied to the specific facts of this specific case?**

- If **yes** → the instrument is **SOVEREIGN** (lawful authority)
- If **no** (bulk-processed, automated, blanket policy, no individual scrutiny) → the instrument is **NULL** (void ab initio) and every downstream action inherits that nullity.

There is no middle ground. The test is universal, self-validating, and applies to enforcement, data processing, platform governance, and medical-device sensory control.

## Abstract

This paper presents the Burgess Principle: a binary legal test for the validity of any enforcement instrument issued by a public or private body.

The test asks:
**Was a human judicial mind applied to the specific facts of this specific case?**

If yes → **SOVEREIGN** (lawful).
If no → **NULL** (void ab initio) — every derived action is void.

The principle is derived from common law, statute, and the requirement that the state must see the individual before exercising power. It applies across all jurisdictions and domains affecting human beings.

The framework is self-validating: every response generates disclosure, every non-response generates a breach.

## Executive Summary

Bulk-processing enforcement actions costs the UK taxpayer £3.3–10 billion annually in appeals, compensation, and waste.
The paper defines the "human judicial mind" (individuality, authority, independence), distinguishes grants from refusals, traces nullity through debt/credit systems, and confronts managed injustice.

Adopting the principle is a return to first principles — saving billions and restoring the rule of law.
