## Background

The complainant — a disabled individual with bilateral sensorineural hearing loss — filed a complaint with the EHRC regarding systematic disability discrimination by energy suppliers. The core evidence was:

- A written admission from a Legal Team Manager at a Magistrates’ Court confirming that warrant applications from energy suppliers are submitted in bulk via CSV file and granted en bloc — without individual judicial consideration
- An unsigned warrant issued and executed by forced entry against a disabled person
- No Equality Act assessment carried out at any stage
- No reasonable adjustments considered
- The underlying dispute concerned a meter, not a debt

## The Four-Email Pattern

### Email 1 — EHRC Template Refusal

The EHRC responded with a standard template:

- We are not resourced to advise individuals
- We focus on a small number of test cases that advance or clarify the law
- Contact the Equality Advisory and Support Service (EASS)

**Analysis:** This is the default brush-off. It is designed to close the file. Most complainants stop here.

### Email 2 — Challenge on Their Own Criteria

The complainant did not accept the referral. Instead, the response reframed the complaint against the EHRC’s own stated test case criteria:

- Test cases are for cases with the potential to advance or clarify the law ✓
- No court has ever ruled on whether bulk algorithmic warrant processing complies with the Equality Act 2010 ✓
- The process by design cannot account for disability, cannot apply reasonable adjustments, and operates without human judicial oversight ✓
- This is not an individual complaint — it is documented evidence of a systemic process affecting every disabled person subject to it ✓

The email closed with: *“If a confirmed bulk algorithmic warrant process affecting disabled consumers across England and Wales does not meet your threshold for a test case that could advance or clarify the law, I would welcome your explanation of what would.”*

**Principle applied:** Do not accept referral loops. Use the institution’s own published criteria against its own refusal.

### Email 3 — EHRC First Confirmation (Accidental Admission)

The EHRC responded:

- Test cases are for gaps or uncertainty in the law
- **“Your case fits the definition of disability discrimination”**
- You need a judge to agree on the discrimination verdict in court
- We do not provide legal services

**Analysis:** The EHRC attempted to close the file but in doing so confirmed in writing that the case meets the legal definition of disability discrimination. This is an institutional admission from the statutory body Parliament created to be the authority on equality law. The confirmation was incidental to their refusal — they did not intend it as a formal finding. That does not diminish its evidential weight.

Their logic also contained a contradiction: they stated test cases are for gaps in the law, then said the case fits existing law. But no court has tested existing law against this specific systemic process. The absence of precedent IS a gap — by their own definition.

### Email 4 — Contradiction Challenge

The complainant’s response did three things:

1. Thanked the EHRC for the written confirmation and noted it would be retained
1. Identified the contradiction: case law vs legislation are not the same thing
1. Put the test case question directly: an untested systemic process is, by definition, a test case

**Principle applied:** When an institution contradicts itself, pin the contradiction precisely and return it as a question they must either answer or document their refusal to answer.

### Email 5 — EHRC Second Confirmation

The EHRC responded again:

- **“The Equality Act would fit your case”**
- You need to seek a lawyer and go through the court system
- Your case would not prompt any legal change
- We are unable to help further

**Analysis:** Second written confirmation. The EHRC also conflated the legislation existing with the legislation being judicially tested against this process — the same error, repeated. They did not engage with the case law distinction.

### Email 6 — Close the File

The complainant’s final email:

1. Noted the second confirmation
1. Corrected the record: the gap is in case law, not legislation
1. Listed the three outcomes secured (case reference, two confirmations, documented refusal)
1. Closed correspondence on the complainant’s terms

## Strategic Value

### As Evidence

The EHRC is the statutory body created by Parliament under the Equality Act 2006 (ss.8, 11) to promote and enforce equality law. Its opinion on whether conduct constitutes discrimination under the Equality Act 2010 is inherently relevant in any proceedings. A court or tribunal is not bound by it, but it carries persuasive weight as the assessment of the competent statutory authority.

### As a Pattern

The EHRC’s refusal to act on confirmed discrimination is itself evidence of the structural gap the Burgess Principle documents. The statutory body recognises the harm and structurally abandons the citizen it was designed to protect. That gap — between recognition and enforcement — is the space the framework operates in.

### Across Fronts

The standard inclusion line can be deployed in correspondence with:

- Energy Ombudsman proceedings
- Local Government Ombudsman proceedings
- Regulatory complaints (Ofgem, ICO)
- Local authority complaints involving Equality Act failures
- Enforcement agent challenges
- Any future HMCTS or tribunal proceedings

Any institution that dismisses it is on record dismissing the view of the national equality regulator on a question of equality law.

## Replicable Method

This pattern is available to any citizen facing algorithmic or bulk-processed enforcement where disability is a factor:

1. **File the EHRC complaint** with specific evidence of a systemic process that cannot account for disability
1. **Reject the template refusal** by reframing against their own test case criteria
1. **Capture the admission** — any language confirming the case “fits” discrimination is an institutional confirmation
1. **Pin any contradiction** between their criteria and their refusal
1. **Close the file on your terms** — list what was secured, note it will be cited, withdraw

The EHRC’s template process is designed to deflect. It is not designed to withstand a complainant who reads their own criteria back to them and asks them to explain the contradiction. Four emails. Two confirmations. One deployable line across every front.
