# Damages Matrix
## The Burgess Principle — Quantification Framework
### UK Certification Mark UK00004343685 | Version 1.0 | April 2026

---

## Overview

This matrix provides a structured methodology for quantifying harm arising from automated institutional decisions that lacked meaningful human involvement. It is a Stage 2 tool — it applies after a Stage 1 declaration has been obtained confirming the defendant's process was automated without adequate review.

The matrix operates across four harm categories. Individual claimants may fall into one or more categories. Harm categories are cumulative — a single claimant who experienced forced entry, a resulting CCJ, and credit damage may recover across all four.

---

## Category 1 — Direct Enforcement Harm

Harm arising directly from the automated enforcement action itself.

| Harm Type | Evidence Required | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Unlawful entry / forced access | Police records, contractor invoices, photographic evidence, witness statements | Trespass, breach of Article 8 ECHR, Equality Act 2010 |
| Property damage during entry | Photographic evidence, repair invoices | Trespass to goods / land |
| Goods removed or meters changed | Inventory records, meter audit | Wrongful interference with goods |
| Personal injury (physical or psychiatric) | Medical records, GP notes, psychiatric report | Negligence / trespass to the person |
| Distress and anxiety (deaf / disabled claimant) | Medical records, correspondence, witness statements | Equality Act 2010 s.119, Article 8 ECHR |

**Indicative range:** £2,000–£25,000 per claimant depending on severity and duration.

**Note on disability uplift:** Where the claimant is on the Priority Services Register, has a documented reasonable adjustment requirement, or has a disability within the meaning of Equality Act 2010 s.6, the distress element attracts an uplift. Document the PSR status, the adjustment request, and the institutional response at the outset.

---

## Category 2 — Credit and Financial Harm

Harm arising from automated data processing and credit reporting based on void or unlawfully obtained enforcement instruments.

| Harm Type | Evidence Required | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| CCJ registered on void liability order | Court records, credit file SAR | UK GDPR Art.22C, DPA 2018 s.168 |
| Credit score damage | Credit file SAR (Experian / Equifax / TransUnion), before and after comparison | UK GDPR Art.22C, misuse of personal data |
| Higher borrowing costs (mortgage / loan rate differential) | Loan offer letters, rate comparison evidence | Consequential financial loss |
| Refused credit applications | Rejection letters, credit file entries | Consequential financial loss |
| Enforcement costs charged on void instruments | Bailiff receipts, enforcement agent correspondence | Unjust enrichment, void instrument basis |

**Quantification method:**
- For credit score damage: calculate the rate differential on any borrowing taken during the period of damage and project over the loan term
- For refused credit: document the specific application and the refusal reason citing the registered default or CCJ
- For enforcement costs: total all amounts paid to enforcement agents under the void instrument chain

**Indicative range:** £1,000–£50,000+ per claimant depending on financial profile and period of damage.

---

## Category 3 — Data Processing Harm

Harm arising from automated processing of personal data without meaningful human involvement, independent of the enforcement outcome.

| Harm Type | Evidence Required | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Processing without Article 22C safeguards | SAR response, defendant's policy documents | UK GDPR Art.22C, DUAA 2025 s.80 |
| Failure to provide right to contest | Correspondence record, SAR | UK GDPR Art.22C(2)(d) |
| Failure to provide human intervention mechanism | Correspondence record, institutional response | UK GDPR Art.22C(2)(c) |
| Data shared with third parties on void basis | SAR, credit bureau SARs, debt collector correspondence | UK GDPR Art.22C, Art.5(1)(a) accuracy |
| Retention of inaccurate data beyond correction | SAR, correction requests and responses | UK GDPR Art.16, Art.17 |

**Quantification method:** Non-material damages for data processing harm are available under UK GDPR Art.82 / DPA 2018 s.168. Follow the approach in *Vidal-Hall v Google* [2015] EWCA Civ 311 and subsequent cases. The German Federal Court (Case VI ZR 10/24, November 2024) awarded damages for loss of control per se — note this as persuasive authority for the proposition that mere loss of control has compensable value, though English courts have been more cautious.

**Indicative range:** £500–£5,000 per claimant for data processing harm alone.

---

## Category 4 — Systemic Impact and Aggregated Harm

Harm arising from the systemic nature of the automated process across the class.

This category applies at Stage 1 level — it informs the scope of declaratory relief and the basis for any injunctive remedies sought.

| Impact Type | Evidence Required | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Number of class members affected | Defendant's own records, disclosed via SAR / FOI | Scope of declaration |
| Period of non-compliance | Process documentation, start and end dates | Duration of breach |
| Regulatory non-compliance | ICO findings, FCA referral outcomes, Ofgem correspondence | Regulatory basis for declaration |
| Failure to implement DUAA safeguards | Post-5 February 2026 correspondence | Article 22C UK GDPR (as amended) |

## Methodology Notes

**Lowest common denominator approach:** For Stage 1, use the claimant with the weakest individual position to stress-test the class definition. If even the lowest-harm claimant has a viable Article 22C claim, the class is solid.

**Bifurcation:** This matrix applies at Stage 2. Do not introduce quantum evidence at Stage 1. The Stage 1 declaration is the gateway — this matrix quantifies what flows through it.

**Disability uplift:** Always document PSR status, reasonable adjustment requests, and institutional responses at the earliest opportunity. These are not afterthoughts — they are the highest-value elements of the individual claim and the most powerful evidence of systemic Equality Act failure.

**Expert evidence:** For credit harm calculations, obtain a financial expert report. For psychiatric harm, obtain a medical report. For technical process analysis, obtain a systems expert who can confirm whether meaningful human involvement was structurally possible within the defendant's architecture.

## Required Documents for Each Claimant (Stage 2)

- [ ] SAR from the defendant (confirming data processed and process used)
- [ ] SAR from all credit bureaus (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion)
- [ ] Copy of any CCJ, liability order, or enforcement notice
- [ ] Correspondence record with defendant (all enforcement communications)
- [ ] PSR registration documentation (if applicable)
- [ ] Reasonable adjustment requests and institutional responses (if applicable)
- [ ] Medical records (if psychiatric or physical harm claimed)
- [ ] Financial records (loan applications, rejection letters, rate evidence)
- [ ] Witness statements (for entry events, enforcement visits)
- [ ] Photographic evidence (for property damage)
