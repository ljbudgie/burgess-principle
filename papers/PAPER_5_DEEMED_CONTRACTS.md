# Paper 5: The Deemed Contract — How Statute Replaced Consent and Machines Replaced Scrutiny

**Lewis James Burgess**
**13 April 2026**

-----

## Abstract

Every energy and water enforcement case in this repository traces back to the same legal root node: the deemed contract. Under Schedule 2B, paragraph 8 of the Gas Act 1986 and Schedule 6, paragraph 3 of the Electricity Act 1989, a binding contractual relationship is created by statute the moment a supply begins — without offer, acceptance, signature, or consent. The supplier then sets the terms unilaterally. This paper argues that the deemed contract is not merely a background legal mechanism but the foundational architecture that enables automated enforcement at scale. When Parliament created deemed contracts, it assumed that the supplier would correctly identify the consumer or occupier before the enforcement pipeline ran. That assumption has collapsed. What operates in practice is a three-layer system in which contract is created without consent, identity is assigned without verification, and enforcement proceeds without review. The Burgess Principle does not challenge Parliament’s right to create deemed contracts. It asks whether the institutions relying on them are verifying the factual preconditions before the machinery runs. The evidence, drawn from every completed and active case in this repository, is that they are not.

-----

## 1. The Legal Architecture

A contract, in its ordinary meaning, requires agreement. Offer, acceptance, consideration, and intention to create legal relations. These are the foundations taught in every first-year law course, inherited from centuries of common law, and understood intuitively by anyone who has ever shaken hands on a deal. You agree, then you are bound.

The deemed contract overrides all of this by statute.

Schedule 2B, paragraph 8(1) of the Gas Act 1986 provides:

> *“Where a gas supplier supplies gas to a consumer otherwise than in pursuance of a contract, the supplier shall be deemed to have contracted with the consumer for the supply of gas as from the time (‘the relevant time’) when he began so to supply gas to the consumer.”*

Schedule 6, paragraph 3(1) of the Electricity Act 1989 provides the equivalent for electricity:

> *“Where an electricity supplier supplies electricity to any premises otherwise than in pursuance of a contract, the supplier shall be deemed to have contracted with the occupier (or the owner if the premises are unoccupied) for the supply of electricity as from the time (‘the relevant time’) when he began so to supply electricity.”*

There is no signature. No phone call. No click-through. No meeting of minds. The contract exists because Parliament says it does, and it exists from the moment the supply begins — which may be before the person living at the property even knows who their supplier is.

As Sir Terence Etherton C confirmed in *Laverty v British Gas Trading Ltd* [2014] EWHC 2721 (Ch), at paragraph 68: such a deemed contract commences from the moment that gas and electricity are supplied to any premises otherwise than pursuant to an actual contract.

There is a logic to this. Parliament did not want people to consume energy without paying for it. Someone must be liable. But the question the statute answers — *someone must pay* — is not the same as the question the system must answer in practice: *who?*

## 2. The Terms Are Not Yours Either

The deemed contract does not merely impose a relationship without consent. It imposes terms without negotiation.

Schedule 2B, paragraph 8(7) of the Gas Act 1986 provides that the express terms and conditions of a deemed contract shall be provided for by a scheme made by the supplier. Etherton C in *Laverty* described this as operating within “a very light and loose regulatory framework” that grants the supplier the ability to determine the precise terms, duration, and means of termination.

In practice, this means the supplier writes the contract, names the customer, sets the price, and begins billing — all without the customer’s knowledge, let alone agreement.

Standard Licence Condition 7.3 requires that deemed contract terms must not be “unduly onerous.” But Ofgem’s own analysis has found that deemed contract rates can be dramatically higher than negotiated rates. In its guidance on deemed contracts (November 2023), Ofgem noted that an information request had revealed typical micro-business electricity bills under deemed contracts were, on average, 82% higher than a new fixed-term contract.

For domestic consumers the gap may be smaller, but the principle is the same: you are bound to terms you did not agree to, at a price you did not negotiate, on an account you may not know exists.

## 3. The Three-Layer Problem

The Burgess Principle exposes a structural failure that operates across three layers. Each layer is individually defensible in isolation. Together, they create a system in which a person can go from unknown to the supplier to having a County Court Judgment on their credit file without any human being ever checking the facts.

### Layer 1: Contract Without Consent

This is the legal foundation. Parliament created it. It is the law. The Burgess Principle does not dispute that deemed contracts are lawful. What it identifies is the downstream consequence: because the contract arises by operation of statute rather than by agreement, there is no natural checkpoint at which the person being bound has any opportunity to verify — or even discover — the obligation being imposed on them.

In a negotiated contract, the act of agreeing is itself a form of review. You see the terms. You know the counterparty. You can walk away. In a deemed contract, that checkpoint does not exist. The first time many people learn they have a deemed contract is when they receive a bill — or worse, an enforcement letter.

### Layer 2: Identity Without Verification

The statute says the deemed contract is with “the consumer” (gas) or “the occupier or owner” (electricity). But who decides who that person is?

In practice, the supplier relies on automated data matching. Credit reference agencies. Industry databases. Batch processes. The Northumbrian Water SAR disclosed in this repository shows an account created on 31 August 2023 using a TransUnion credit data batch, with a move-in date of 1 April 2023 assumed from that data. The internal note reads: *“Account and services setup from Trans Union credit data via batch. Letter sent.”*

No one checked whether the person identified was actually the occupier. No one verified the move-in date. No one confirmed the tenancy or ownership status. The system identified a name, attached it to an address, and started billing.

This is not an aberration. This is the standard process. It is how accounts are created at scale across the energy and water sectors. The statute assumes the supplier knows who the consumer is. The supplier assumes the data is correct. Nobody checks.

### Layer 3: Enforcement Without Review

Once the account exists and the bills go unpaid — often because the person does not know the account exists — the enforcement pipeline runs automatically:

1. Bills accumulate.
1. Reminder letters are sent (often to an address, not to a verified individual).
1. Pre-claim letters follow.
1. A claim is filed at the County Court.
1. If no defence is filed (because the person may not have received the claim, or may not understand it, or may not know they are the named party), judgment is entered by default.
1. The CCJ appears on the person’s credit file.
1. Enforcement agents are instructed.

At no point in this sequence is a human being with authority required to ask: *Is this the right person? Is this debt valid? Were the factual preconditions for the deemed contract actually met?*

The deemed contract gave the supplier statutory permission to charge the occupier. But the pipeline never verified who the occupier was. The enforcement proceeded on the basis of an assumption fed into an automated system, and the system did what systems do: it ran.

## 4. The Water Sector Parallel

Water operates under a different statutory framework — the Water Industry Act 1991 — but the practical architecture is identical. Water undertakers are monopoly suppliers. There is no switching. The relationship arises by virtue of the property being connected to the water network. The consumer has even less agency than in energy, because there is no alternative supplier to choose.

The Northumbrian Water case in this repository demonstrates the pattern in its purest form. An account was created by batch data process. The occupancy status was recorded as “Tenant” and the number of occupiers as “> 4” — neither verified. The enforcement pipeline ran from first bill (December 2023) through pre-claim letter (July 2024) to claim execution (October 2024) to judgment by default (December 2024). The billing basis was subsequently corrected downward in February 2025 — after the CCJ had already been entered.

The entire sequence, from account creation to judgment, operated without a single point at which a human being examined whether the charges were correctly attributed to the right person.

## 5. The Warrant Extension

In the energy sector, the deemed contract is also the legal basis on which suppliers seek warrants to enter domestic premises. Under Schedule 2B, paragraph 11 of the Gas Act 1986 and the Rights of Entry (Gas and Electricity Boards) Act 1954, a supplier can apply to a Justice of the Peace for a warrant authorising forced entry.

The E.ON case in this repository (EG021819-26) demonstrates what happens when this power intersects with the same automated pipeline. An unsigned warrant — Application 11160-544079, issued at Birmingham Magistrates’ Court on 16 May 2025 — authorised forced entry to a domestic property. The warrant bore no signature of any named Justice of the Peace. Three magistrates’ names were embedded in the document metadata but were not visible on the face of the warrant itself.

Andrew Fletcher, Justices’ Legal Adviser at Birmingham Magistrates’ Court, subsequently confirmed the process by which such warrants are granted: CSV file applications, generic hearings, 5% case sampling, en bloc grants. This is bulk warrant processing. The deemed contract provides the legal foundation. The automated pipeline identifies the premises. The warrant application is generated. And the court processes it in batch.

The binary question remains: *Was a human judicial mind individually applied to the specific facts of this specific person’s case before the warrant was granted?* The Andrew Fletcher admission answers it.

## 7. The Doctrinal Position

The Burgess Principle does not argue that deemed contracts are unlawful. Parliament created them for a legitimate purpose: ensuring that energy consumed is paid for. The principle does not challenge the statute. It challenges the institutions that rely on it.

The binary test — *Was a human judicial mind individually applied to the specific facts of this specific person’s case?* — is not a test of legislative validity. It is a test of institutional conduct. And when applied to the enforcement pipelines built on top of deemed contracts, it exposes a systematic failure: the factual preconditions that the statute assumes — that the supplier has correctly identified the consumer or occupier — are never individually verified before the enforcement machinery activates.

This is the gap. The statute says: *someone must pay.* The system says: *this person must pay.* And nobody checks whether those two statements point to the same individual.

-----

## 8. The Deemed Contract as Root Node

In software engineering, a root node is the top-level element from which all other elements descend. In the tree of automated energy and water enforcement, the deemed contract is the root node.

Every bill, every reminder, every pre-claim letter, every claim, every default judgment, every warrant application, every enforcement agent visit, and every credit file entry descends from the deemed contract. If the root node is sound — if the identity is correct, the supply is verified, and the terms are fair — then the tree may be lawful, even if the process is automated. But if the root node is flawed — if the identity is assumed, the supply is unverified, and the terms are imposed without notice — then every branch of the tree inherits the flaw.

The Andrew Fletcher admission — CSV files, generic hearings, 5% sampling, en bloc grants — is not an aberration in the system. It is the system working as designed. The deemed contract provides the legal basis for the pipeline. The pipeline is built for volume. And volume requires automation. The human being was removed from the process not by accident but by architecture.

-----

## 9. What Would SOVEREIGN Look Like?

If the deemed contract is the root node, what would a SOVEREIGN version of the process require?

It would require that before any enforcement action is taken — before the first pre-claim letter, before the claim is filed, before the warrant is applied for — a named human being with proper authority individually reviews the specific case and confirms:

1. The person identified as the consumer or occupier is in fact the consumer or occupier.
1. The supply period attributed to that person is correct.
1. The billing is accurate and based on verified data (meter readings, not estimates).
1. Any vulnerability, disability, or reasonable adjustment requirement has been identified and accommodated.
1. The enforcement action is proportionate to the circumstances.

This is not an unreasonable standard. It is, in fact, what the statute assumes is already happening. The deemed contract provisions were drafted on the assumption that suppliers know who their customers are. The Burgess Principle simply asks them to prove it.

-----

## 10. Closing

The deemed contract is the most powerful legal instrument in the domestic enforcement landscape. It creates obligations without consent, imposes terms without negotiation, and activates pipelines without review. It was designed by Parliament to ensure fairness — that energy consumed is paid for. But in the hands of institutions operating at scale, it has become the legal foundation of a system that enforces first and checks later, if at all.

Every case in this repository answers the same binary question. And in every case involving energy or water, the trail leads back to the same root node. The deemed contract is not the villain of this story. The villain is the assumption — embedded in the statute, exploited by the system — that someone, somewhere, is checking the facts.

Nobody is.
