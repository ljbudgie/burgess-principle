# Warrant Defect Identifier
## The Burgess Principle — Enforcement Warrant Assessment Tool
### UK Certification Mark UK00004343685 | Version 1.0 | April 2026

---

## Purpose

This tool assesses whether an entry warrant issued under the Rights of Entry (Gas and Electricity Boards) Act 1954 — or equivalent enforcement instrument — is void ab initio due to facial defect or procedural invalidity arising from automated batch processing.

A warrant that is void ab initio never existed in law. Every enforcement action built upon it — forced entry, meter installation, bailiff instruction, credit reporting, CCJ registration — inherits that nullity.

---

## Step 1 — Obtain the Warrant

Request a copy of the warrant via:
- Subject Access Request to the energy company (they hold the application)
- FOI request to the relevant Magistrates' Court
- Direct request to the court under the open justice principle

If the warrant cannot be produced, that is itself significant — document the failure to produce.

---

## Step 2 — Facial Defect Checklist

Examine the face of the warrant against each requirement:

### Mandatory Elements

| Element | Required | Present? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Judicial signature | Yes — must be signed by a Justice of the Peace or District Judge | ☐ Yes ☐ No | An unsigned warrant is void ab initio |
| Named justice | Yes — name of signing justice must be legible | ☐ Yes ☐ No | Anonymous signature is insufficient |
| Named occupier / address | Yes — warrant must identify the specific premises | ☐ Yes ☐ No | Generic or batch-addressed warrants are defective |
| Date of issue | Yes | ☐ Yes ☐ No | |
| Court seal | Yes — official court seal required | ☐ Yes ☐ No | |
| Statutory basis cited | Yes — Rights of Entry Act 1954 s.2(1) | ☐ Yes ☐ No | |
| Specific authorisation | Yes — must authorise specific acts at specific premises | ☐ Yes ☐ No | |

**If any mandatory element is absent: the warrant is void ab initio.**

### Procedural Validity Checklist

| Question | Check |
|---|---|
| Was the warrant granted at a hearing where the occupier had the opportunity to be heard? | ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Unknown |
| Was the warrant granted as part of a batch hearing (multiple cases listed simultaneously)? | ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Unknown |
| Was the warrant generated by an automated or algorithmic process prior to judicial sign-off? | ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Unknown |
| Did the issuing justice individually review the facts of this specific property? | ☐ Yes ☐ No ☐ Unknown |
| Was the applicant (energy company) on the Priority Services Register for this address? | ☐ Yes ☐ No |
| Was the occupier deaf, disabled, or otherwise requiring reasonable adjustment? | ☐ Yes ☐ No |

**If batch processing is confirmed: apply the Burgess Principle binary test to the judicial process itself.**

The binary test asks: *"Was a human member of the team [the issuing justice] able to personally review the specific facts of this person's situation?"*

If warrants were processed in CSV batches — as Birmingham Magistrates' Court has admitted in writing — the answer is NULL. No individual judicial mind was applied to the specific facts. The warrant is void.

---

## Step 3 — Obtain the Court Admission

The most powerful evidence is a written admission from the court itself. In the Burgess Principle audit, the following admission was obtained from Birmingham Magistrates' Court:

> *"The Court confirms that energy warrants are processed in batches. [Specific number] warrants were granted in [period]. The process involves CSV upload of applications."*

*(Exact wording held in the audit log — request from the originator under Tier 4 licence.)*

To obtain the equivalent admission for your specific court:

1. Write to the Justices' Legal Adviser at the relevant Magistrates' Court
2. Ask specifically: how many energy warrants were granted in [period]? What was the process by which individual warrants were considered before being granted?
3. Any response confirming batch processing or volume processing without individual consideration is a usable admission

## Step 4 — Contamination Chain

Once a warrant is identified as void, trace the contamination chain:

```
VOID WARRANT
    │
    ├── Forced entry authorised under void warrant
    │       → Trespass claim
    │       → Article 8 ECHR claim (right to home)
    │       → Equality Act claim (if PSR / disability)
    │
    ├── Meter installation / equipment change under void warrant
    │       → Wrongful interference with goods
    │       → Unjust enrichment (equipment costs)
    │
    ├── Enforcement costs charged under void warrant
    │       → Recovery of all enforcement agent fees paid
    │
    ├── Data processed and shared on basis of void warrant
    │       → UK GDPR Art.22C, Art.5(1)(a) (accuracy)
    │       → ICO complaint basis
    │
    ├── CCJ registered on basis of void liability order
    │       → Credit harm (see Damages Matrix Category 2)
    │       → Application to set aside CCJ
    │
    └── Credit bureau data (Experian / Equifax / TransUnion)
            → SAR to all three bureaus
            → Article 16 UK GDPR correction request
            → Article 17 UK GDPR erasure request
            → If refused: ICO complaint + damages claim
```

## Step 5 — Immediate Actions

Once defect is confirmed:

1. **Write to the energy company** — formal notice that the warrant was void ab initio, all enforcement under it was unlawful, and a claim will follow if not remedied within 14 days.

2. **Write to all credit bureaus** — Article 16 correction request citing the void warrant as the basis for any adverse data. Require removal within one month.

3. **Write to the ICO** — complaint that the energy company processed personal data on the basis of a void instrument, in breach of the accuracy principle (Art.5(1)(a)) and without lawful basis.

4. **Write to the court** — formal challenge to the warrant's validity, requesting confirmation of the process by which it was issued.

5. **File a pre-action protocol letter** — to the energy company and (if applicable) the court, setting out the claim and requesting remedy within the protocol period.

Use the correspondence templates in the Correspondence Packs for all of the above.

## Applicable Law

- Rights of Entry (Gas and Electricity Boards) Act 1954, s.2(1) — warrant requirements
- Magistrates' Courts Act 1980 — judicial function and warrant validity
- UK GDPR Art.5(1)(a) — accuracy principle
- UK GDPR Art.22A–22C (as amended by DUAA 2025) — automated decisions
- Equality Act 2010 s.20, s.29 — reasonable adjustments for service providers
- Human Rights Act 1998, Article 8 ECHR — right to home and private life
- Protection from Harassment Act 1997 — where repeated enforcement targeting is documented
