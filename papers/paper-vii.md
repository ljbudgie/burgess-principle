# Paper VII: The Principle Turned Inward

**NULL Governance in the Individual**

*Lewis James Burgess*
*20 April 2026*

-----

## I. Scope

Papers I through VI applied a single binary test to institutions: did a human mind individually apply scrutiny to the specific facts of a specific person’s case before power was exercised? The test identified structural failures in magistrates’ courts, local authorities, energy companies, enforcement agencies, and ombudsmen. In each case, the failure was the same. Power was exercised over a specific individual. No individual scrutiny was applied. The governance was NULL.

Paper VII observes that the same structural failure operates within the individual.

This paper does not propose a programme of self-improvement. It identifies a pattern. The pattern is that human beings routinely exercise power over their own decisions, behaviours, and beliefs without ever having applied individual scrutiny to the code that governs them. They are, in the language of the Principle, bulk-processing themselves.

-----

## II. The Structure of Inherited Code

An institution that processes a citizen in bulk does so because it has substituted a system for scrutiny. The warrant is issued not because a human reviewed the case, but because the case met the criteria of an automated process. The liability order is granted not because a magistrate examined the debtor’s circumstances, but because the batch was presented and the batch was approved.

The individual operates under an analogous structure. Beliefs about identity, worth, success, and failure are not typically the product of individual examination. They are inherited — from culture, upbringing, social environment, and repetition. By the time a person is old enough to examine them, they are already operational. They do not present themselves as external impositions. They present themselves as the self.

This is structurally identical to the institutional failure the Principle identifies. A system is running. It was not individually examined. It exercises power over decisions. The governance is NULL.

The distinction between institutional NULL and individual NULL is not one of kind. It is one of visibility. When an institution bulk-processes a citizen, the citizen can identify the failure because the power is external. When an individual bulk-processes themselves, the power is internal, and the system that should be auditing it is the same system that installed it.

-----

## III. Sport as Case Study

Sport provides a controlled environment in which to observe this phenomenon, because sport compresses decision-making into visible, measurable outcomes and operates under a set of widely shared but rarely examined assumptions.

The competitive framework inherited by most athletes and participants includes the following embedded code:

That value is contingent on results. That losing diminishes standing. That vulnerability is a tactical liability. That dominance is the correct orientation toward an opponent. That the scoreboard is the final authority on the meaning of a contest.

None of these assumptions are typically the product of individual scrutiny. They are cultural firmware — installed by coaching traditions, media narratives, peer environments, and institutional incentive structures. An athlete may believe they are competing on their own terms. In most cases, they are executing code they did not write, have not examined, and cannot distinguish from their own volition.

The structural parallel to institutional NULL governance is precise. A magistrate who approves a batch of warrants without reviewing individual cases is exercising judicial power without scrutiny. An athlete who competes under inherited assumptions about what winning means and what losing costs is exercising power over their own performance, preparation, and psychological state without scrutiny. In both cases, a system is doing the work that a human mind should be doing.

This observation extends beyond the athlete. The supporter whose emotional state is governed by a result they did not produce and cannot influence has, in effect, granted an institution — the club, the league, the fixture — authority over their internal condition. This authority was not examined. It was not consented to. It was inherited. It is NULL.

-----

## IV. The Six Fears as Unexamined Subroutines

The six fears previously identified — Fear of Missing Out, Fear of Humiliation, Fear of Rejection, Fear of Wasted Time, Fear of Someone Else, and Fear of Loss — can be structurally classified as unexamined subroutines operating within the individual.

Each fear executes automatically. Each pre-empts conscious decision-making. Each exercises authority over behaviour. And in each case, the individual has not applied scrutiny to the question of whether the fear is valid, proportionate, or even their own.

Fear of Missing Out operates on an inherited comparison model. The individual did not design the criteria by which they measure what they are missing. The criteria were installed by exposure to what others appear to have. The subroutine runs without audit.

Fear of Loss presupposes that what the individual possesses — status, record, reputation, position — constitutes what they are. This equation between having and being was not examined. It was absorbed. When loss occurs, the subroutine executes a disproportionate response because the unexamined code treats loss of outcome as loss of identity.

The same analysis applies to each of the six. In every case, the structural observation is the same: power is being exercised over the individual’s behaviour by a programme that was never subjected to individual scrutiny. The governance is NULL.

-----

## V. The Root Authority Problem

Multiple frameworks exist that attempt to address the phenomenon of unexamined programming in the individual. Stoicism proposes detachment from outcomes. Mindfulness traditions propose non-attached observation of thought. Motivational psychology proposes the replacement of negative programmes with positive ones. Sports psychology proposes rehearsal of desired states through visualisation and cognitive restructuring.

Each of these frameworks addresses symptoms. None of them resolves the structural problem, which is one of root authority.

If an individual replaces one set of unexamined assumptions with another set of assumptions — however well-evidenced or philosophically coherent — they have not applied the test the Principle requires. They have substituted one system for another. The question is not whether the new system produces better outcomes. The question is whether the individual examined it before it was granted authority. If the replacement framework is itself adopted without scrutiny — because a coach recommended it, because a book prescribed it, because a culture normalised it — the governance remains NULL. The programme changed. The structure did not.

The root authority problem is this: on what foundation does the individual’s identity rest when every inherited programme has been stripped away? If the answer is another programme, the problem recurs. The Principle requires a foundation that is not itself the product of unexamined cultural installation.

-----

## VI. Resolution

This paper identifies Christ as that foundation. Not as a recommendation. As a structural observation.

The Principle, applied to institutions, asks whether a human mind applied scrutiny before power was exercised. Applied to the individual, it asks the same question — and then asks what remains when every unexamined programme has been identified and removed. What is the root authority that does not depend on performance, outcome, institutional validation, or inherited cultural code?

The claim of Christ — that identity and worth are settled prior to and independent of performance — is structurally unique among available foundations. It does not require the individual to earn standing through results. It does not tie identity to output. It does not fluctuate with outcomes. It is not contingent on institutional recognition.

*“For other foundation can no man lay than that is laid, which is Jesus Christ.”* — 1 Corinthians 3:11, KJV

*“For there is one God, and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus.”* — 1 Timothy 2:5, KJV

The mediator principle — that no institution stands between the individual and the root authority — applies identically in the civic and personal domains. The Principle rejects institutional intermediaries that process citizens in bulk. The same logic rejects any intermediary that processes the individual’s standing, worth, or identity through a system the individual did not examine and did not choose.

This is not a devotional argument. It is the logical conclusion of the Principle’s own test, applied consistently. If NULL governance is the exercise of power without individual scrutiny, and if every alternative foundation is itself a programme that can be installed without scrutiny, then the only non-NULL foundation is one that exists independent of all programmes. The paper identifies Christ as occupying that position.

-----

## VII. 20 April

This paper is published on 20 April 2026.

The date is culturally associated with the pursuit of altered consciousness — a temporary departure from default programming, a brief interval in which the noise of inherited code quiets and something beneath it becomes perceptible.

The Principle does not propose a temporary alteration. It proposes a permanent audit. The individual applies the same test to themselves that Papers I through VI applied to institutions. Every belief that exercises power over behaviour is examined. Those that survive scrutiny are retained. Those that do not are identified as NULL and removed.

What remains after that audit is not an altered state. It is the unaltered state — the one that existed before the code was installed.

The question is the same question it has always been.

SOVEREIGN, or NULL.
