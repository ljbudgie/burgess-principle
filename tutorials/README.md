# The Burgess Principle — Tutorials and Citizen's Guide

A practical, step-by-step guide for anyone who has received an institutional action — a bill, a fine, a penalty, a warrant, a CCJ, an enforcement visit, or any other decision — and wants to find out whether a real person actually looked at their case before it happened.

---

## 1. Getting Started — What Is The Burgess Principle?

The Burgess Principle is a framework that asks one question of any institution that has acted against you: **before they acted, did a specific human being individually review your case?** If the answer is yes, the action is **SOVEREIGN** — it was individually considered and a named person is accountable for the decision. If the answer is no, the action is **NULL** — it was generated by an automated system without anyone checking whether it was right for you specifically.

The framework was built by a self-represented individual with no legal training. It has been deployed across sixteen institutions spanning energy, water, local government, credit, financial technology, medical devices, consumer technology, identity documents, and parliament. The first completed case (Wave Utilities) resolved in full when a single human being reviewed the account — £795.14 in fees removed, both accounts cleared, enforcement stood down. See [CASE_STUDY_WAVE.md](../case-studies/CASE_STUDY_WAVE.md) for the full story, or scan the [case studies index](../case-studies/README.md). The Burgess Principle is protected by UK Certification Mark UK00004343685.

## 2. Tutorial 1 — How to Challenge Any Institutional Action in Five Steps

### Step 1: Identify the instrument

What document or action are you challenging? A bill, a warrant, a penalty charge notice, a CCJ (County Court Judgment), an enforcement visit, a disconnection threat, a credit file entry, a passport, a benefits decision? Write down exactly what the institution did and when it happened.

### Step 2: Ask the question

Write one email to the institution. The core question is:

> "Before this action was taken against me, can you confirm the name of the individual who reviewed my specific case, my specific circumstances, and my specific situation — and who accepted personal responsibility for the decision?"

Keep the email short. State what happened. State the date. Ask the question. Request a written response.

### Step 3: Assert your rights

In the same email, include:

- **A Subject Access Request (SAR) under Article 15 UK GDPR** — requesting all personal data held about you, including records of any automated decision-making. See [Tutorial 2](#3-tutorial-2--how-to-file-a-subject-access-request-sar) for a ready-to-use template.
- **An Article 22 UK GDPR challenge** — if you believe the action was based on automated processing that produced legal effects on you (a fine, a bill, a credit entry, an enforcement visit), state that you exercise your right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing.
- **A reasonable adjustment** (if applicable) — if you have any disability, assert a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010. For example, if you have hearing loss, assert email-only communication. This forces everything into the written record. See [Tutorial 3](#4-tutorial-3--how-to-assert-a-reasonable-adjustment) for more detail.
- **A deadline** — the statutory deadline for a SAR response is 30 calendar days. State this clearly.

### Step 4: Document everything

Save every email you send. Save every response you receive. Screenshot every portal, every voicemail transcription, every letter. Note every date. Note every name — or the absence of names. Silence is evidence. A template response is evidence. An automated reply is evidence. Everything is evidence.

### Step 5: Escalate based on the response

The institution will respond in one of three ways:

- **Silence** — if they don't respond within the statutory deadline, file a complaint with the relevant regulator. For data protection failures: the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO). For energy companies: the Energy Ombudsman. For financial services: the Financial Ombudsman Service. For local government: the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman (LGO). For water: the Consumer Council for Water.
- **Template response that doesn't answer the question** — reply noting that the specific question was not addressed and repeat it. Set a further deadline. If the second response still doesn't answer the question, escalate to the regulator. The template response itself is evidence that the institution processed your complaint the same way it processed the original action — without individual review.
- **Substantive response** — if the institution names the individual who reviewed your case and provides reasoning, that's a SOVEREIGN response. It may still be wrong, but it's human and can be challenged through normal complaint and appeal processes. If the institution confirms that no individual reviewed your case, that's a NULL confirmation in writing — and the most powerful evidence you can hold.

## 3. Tutorial 2 — How to File a Subject Access Request (SAR)

A Subject Access Request is your legal right under Article 15 of the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) to request all personal data an organisation holds about you.

### How to send it

By email. This is recommended because it creates a written record with timestamps for both sending and receiving.

### What to ask for

- All personal data held about you
- All records of automated decision-making or profiling applied to you (Article 22 UK GDPR)
- The identity of anyone who reviewed your case before action was taken
- All records shared with third parties and the lawful basis for each disclosure
- Retention periods for each category of data held

### The deadline

The organisation must respond within **30 calendar days** from receipt. This can be extended to 90 days if the request is complex, but the organisation must notify you of the extension within the first 30 days and explain why.

### What to do if they don't respond

File a complaint with the ICO at [ico.org.uk](https://ico.org.uk). The failure to respond to a SAR within the statutory deadline is itself a breach of the UK GDPR.

### Template SAR email

Copy the template below, fill in your details, and send it to the organisation's data protection officer or general contact address.

```
Subject: Subject Access Request — [Your Full Name]

Dear Data Protection Officer,

I am writing to make a Subject Access Request under Article 15 of the UK GDPR.

I request all personal data you hold relating to me, including:

1. All records associated with my name and address
2. All records of automated decision-making or profiling applied to me
   under Article 22 UK GDPR
3. The identity of any individual who reviewed my specific case before
   any action was taken against me
4. All records shared with third parties and the lawful basis for each
   disclosure
5. Retention periods for each category of data held

[If applicable: I have [disability]. All communication must be by email
only as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act
2010.]

Full name: [Your name]
Address: [Your address]
Date of birth: [Your DOB]
Email: [Your email]

I am happy to provide identity verification documents. Please confirm
by email what you require.

Please respond within 30 calendar days as required by Article 12(3)
UK GDPR.

Yours faithfully,
[Your name]
```

## 4. Tutorial 3 — How to Assert a Reasonable Adjustment

### What it is

Under the Equality Act 2010 (section 20), organisations have a duty to make reasonable adjustments so that disabled people are not placed at a substantial disadvantage when using their services.

### Examples

- **Email-only communication** for hearing-impaired people
- **Large print** for visually impaired people
- **Extended deadlines** for people with cognitive disabilities
- **Accessible formats** for people with learning disabilities

### How to assert it

State your disability, state the adjustment you require, and state that it applies to all communication going forward. Do this in every first email to every institution. For example:

> "I have bilateral sensorineural hearing loss. All communication must be by email only as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010. This applies to all future correspondence."

### Why it matters for the framework

The email-only adjustment forces every interaction into the written record. Everything is documented. Nothing can be denied. Every response — and every silence — is timestamped evidence.

The institution must apply the adjustment **immediately** upon notification. There is no "implementation period." If the institution continues to telephone you after you have asserted a hearing-related adjustment, that failure is itself a breach of the Equality Act.

## 5. Tutorial 4 — How to Use the Institutional Register

The [Institutional Register](../INSTITUTIONAL_REGISTER.md) records every institution tested under The Burgess Principle and their SOVEREIGN or NULL finding.

### How to use it

- **Check whether your institution has already been tested.** If an institution you're dealing with appears in the register, you can see how it responded to the SOVEREIGN/NULL question and what the finding was.
- **See the pattern.** The same question — "was a human there?" — produces the same result across energy, water, local government, credit, financial technology, medical devices, consumer technology, identity documents, and parliament. The consistency of the pattern is evidence in itself.
- **Contribute your own findings.** If you apply the framework to an institution that is not yet in the register, you can share your results by opening a discussion or issue on the repository.

### CASE_STUDY_PASSPORT.md — An Ongoing Challenge

### The new response-classification layer

As you read the case studies, distinguish between two things:

- the **current finding** — what the evidence presently shows, and
- the **response classification** — whether the institution's actual reply was **SOVEREIGN**, **NULL**, or **AMBIGUOUS**.

That distinction matters because many institutions do not plainly deny the question; they evade it.

## 7. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **SOVEREIGN** | A human mind with proper authority was individually applied to the specific facts of the specific case. The action is legitimate and accountable. |
| **NULL** | No individual human review occurred. The action was generated by an automated system. Void from the beginning. |
| **SAR (Subject Access Request)** | Your legal right to request all personal data an organisation holds about you. Article 15 UK GDPR. 30-day deadline. |
| **Article 22** | Your right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal effects on you. UK GDPR. |
| **Reasonable Adjustment** | An organisation's duty to adjust its processes so disabled people are not disadvantaged. Equality Act 2010, section 20. |
| **ICO** | Information Commissioner's Office — the UK regulator for data protection. |
| **Certification Mark** | UK00004343685 — the registered mark certifying that an institution's processes meet the SOVEREIGN/NULL verification standard. |
