# Frequently Asked Questions

## What is the Burgess Principle?

The Burgess Principle is a certified legal-accountability framework that applies a single binary test to any institutional decision affecting a specific person: **“Was a human member of the team able to personally review the specific facts of my specific situation?”**

The answer is either **SOVEREIGN** (yes — a named human being individually reviewed the case) or **NULL** (no — the decision was processed automatically without individual human consideration).

The framework was created by Lewis James Burgess, a deaf barber from Darlington, after his home was entered under an unsigned warrant in May 2025. It is published under the MIT licence at [github.com/ljbudgie/burgess-principle](https://github.com/ljbudgie/burgess-principle).

## How does the Burgess Principle relate to the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025?

The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 (DUAA 2025) brought **Articles 22A–22D UK GDPR** into force on **5 February 2026**, replacing the original Article 22. The central statutory standard introduced by Articles 22A–22D is **meaningful human involvement** in automated decisions that produce legal or similarly significant effects on individuals.

The Burgess Principle binary test asks the same question the statute asks: was there meaningful human involvement in this specific decision about this specific person?

The convergence is independent — the binary test was developed from personal necessity before the DUAA 2025 received Royal Assent. The result is that the Burgess Principle and Articles 22A–22D UK GDPR now operate as parallel standards asking the same question. An institution that returns a **NULL** result under the binary test is also failing the statutory requirement for meaningful human involvement.

## What is “meaningful human involvement” under Articles 22A–22D UK GDPR?

Under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, a decision is **solely automated** — and therefore subject to additional safeguards — when it is made with **no meaningful human involvement**. Meaningful human involvement requires more than nominal oversight. It requires a named human being to have personally reviewed the specific facts of the specific case before the decision was made or given effect.

The Burgess Principle operationalises this standard. If an institution cannot name the person who reviewed the case, describe what specific facts they reviewed, and confirm their review took place before the decision was actioned, the binary test result is NULL — and the statutory standard has not been met.

## Is the Burgess Principle pseudolaw or “sovereign citizen” theory?

No. The Burgess Principle does not claim immunity from institutional authority. It does not assert that individuals can opt out of legal jurisdiction. It demands the opposite: that institutional authority be exercised with individual human consideration, which is the same standard required by:

- **Articles 22A–22D UK GDPR** (meaningful human involvement — Data (Use and Access) Act 2025)
- **Equality Act 2010 ss.20–21** (reasonable adjustments for disabled individuals)
- **Common law principles of natural justice** (the right to be heard as an individual)

The word “sovereign” in the Burgess Principle does not mean “exempt from legal authority.” It means “a specific human mind was individually applied.” The framework asks institutions to confirm whether they met their existing legal obligations. It does not create new legal claims — it makes the absence of existing compliance visible and documentable.

## What is the SOVEREIGN/NULL binary test?

The binary test asks one question: **“Was a human member of the team able to personally review the specific facts of my specific situation?”**

- **SOVEREIGN** — yes. A named human being individually reviewed the specific facts before the decision was made or given effect.
- **NULL** — no. The decision was processed automatically without individual human consideration of the specific person.
- **AMBIGUOUS** — the institution responded with vague process language (*“subject to human oversight”*, *“reviewed in line with policy”*) without naming the reviewer or confirming what specific facts were reviewed. This does not answer the question.

An AMBIGUOUS response triggers a follow-up asking for a direct YES or NO plus the reviewer’s name and role. Under Articles 22A–22D UK GDPR, claimed compliance with “meaningful human involvement” without substance does not satisfy the statutory standard.

## How does the Burgess Principle apply to AI systems?

An AI system generating a response to a specific user operates without a named human being personally reviewing the specific facts of that user’s situation before the response is delivered. Applied to itself, any AI system returns a **NULL** result under the binary test.

This is not a criticism — it is an accurate description of the architecture. The framework does not require AI systems to be something they are not. It requires honesty about what they are.

Under Articles 22A–22D UK GDPR, AI-generated decisions that produce legal or similarly significant effects on individuals require meaningful human involvement. AI companies that cannot demonstrate such involvement in specific operations are not certified — and their non-certification is a matter of public record.

## How does the Burgess Principle relate to the Equality Act 2010?

The binary test intersects with the Equality Act 2010 wherever automated decisions affect disabled individuals. Sections 20–21 require reasonable adjustments. Section 15 prohibits discrimination arising from disability. Section 19 prohibits indirect discrimination.

When an institution processes a disabled person through an automated system without individual human review, it is simultaneously failing the Burgess Principle binary test and risking breach of the Equality Act 2010 — because the system cannot apply reasonable adjustments it was never designed to consider.

The framework treats email-only communication as a reasonable adjustment under Equality Act 2010 ss.20 and 29 wherever relevant.
