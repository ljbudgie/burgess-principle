## Abstract

In April 2026, the United States Supreme Court issued a decision requiring the refund of approximately $159 billion in tariff payments collected from importers. Simultaneously, the United States government threatened retaliatory tariffs against United Kingdom businesses in response to the UK's digital services tax — tariffs that would be applied categorically by sector and country of origin. This paper applies the Burgess Principle's binary test — SOVEREIGN or NULL — to both situations. It connects that test to an existing Federal Circuit precedent bearing the same name: *Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board*, 758 F.2d 641 (Fed. Cir. 1985), which established in US administrative law the requirement now formally known as **Burgess notice**: that before institutional power is exercised over an individual, a specific human being must personally review the specific facts of that specific case. The binary test does not take sides. It applies to whoever exercises institutional power through bulk or automated processing without individual human review — whether that is a US agency processing $159 billion in refund claims, or a trade authority imposing categorical tariffs on UK businesses without individual consideration of their specific circumstances. The framework to identify, document, and challenge that failure already exists.

## I. The Federal Precedent: Burgess Notice

In *Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board*, 758 F.2d 641 (Fed. Cir. 1985), the Federal Circuit vacated the MSPB's summary dismissal of an appellant's claim and established a requirement that has since become foundational in US administrative law.

The appellant, Bridgett L. Burgess, had resigned from her position under alleged institutional pressure. The MSPB issued a generic show-cause order and dismissed her claim without a hearing, calling her allegations a "mere conclusion." The Federal Circuit held that this was insufficient. Before an institution denies a person a hearing or a substantive right, it must:

1. Provide clear and specific notice of what that individual must demonstrate
2. Have a specific human being personally consider the specific facts of that specific case
3. Not rely on broad presumptions, formulaic processing, or generic orders as a substitute for individual review

Where jurisdiction and merits are intertwined — where the individual's specific facts are inseparable from the question of whether the right applies — summary processing without proper notice and individual review violates due process.

This requirement is now formally known as **Burgess notice** in US administrative law. It is cited in MSPB Judges' Handbooks, referenced in administrative regulations, and remains active precedent in the Federal Circuit.

The Burgess Principle's binary test arrived at the same standard independently, forty years later, from personal necessity in the United Kingdom. The convergence was not derivative. It demonstrates that the standard is not jurisdiction-specific — it is the standard that independent legal systems arrive at when they reason carefully about what procedural fairness requires before institutional power is exercised over an individual.

## II. The Binary Test

Every exercise of institutional power over an individual resolves to one of two findings:

| Finding | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **SOVEREIGN** | A specific, named human being personally reviewed the specific facts of this specific case before power was exercised. The decision is individually grounded and accountable. |
| **NULL** | No individual human review took place. The decision was generated by category, presumption, algorithm, or bulk processing. Authority is not grounded in personal scrutiny. |

The test is neutral. It does not ask whether the outcome was correct. It asks whether a human being was present at the decision point — before the power was exercised, not after.

## III. Application One: The $159 Billion Tariff Refund Obligation

Following the Supreme Court's decision, US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) is obligated to process refunds of approximately $159 billion in tariff payments to importers across the United States.

Each refund claim represents an individual importer with specific entry records, specific payment amounts, specific reliance interests, specific business circumstances, and specific legal entitlements under the Court's ruling.

The institutional pressure at this scale is toward category-based or automated processing. Batch approvals, algorithmic determinations, and formulaic denials are the path of least resistance when the volume is measured in hundreds of thousands of individual entries.

### The NULL risk for refund denials

Where CBP denies or inadequately processes a refund claim through:

- Automated or algorithmic determination without individual human review
- Category-based denial without examination of specific entry facts
- Formulaic processing without documented consideration of individual reliance interests
- Generic notice without specific instruction on what each importer must demonstrate

— the processing fails the Burgess notice standard. Every importer whose claim is handled this way has a documented basis to demand the name and role of the specific human being who reviewed their specific claim, evidence that their specific entry facts were individually considered, and a hearing if their nonfrivolous allegations of entitlement are not individually addressed.

### The NULL risk for refund approvals

The same standard applies in reverse. Where CBP approves refund claims in bulk without individual verification of each entry record against the specific basis for entitlement, those approvals are equally vulnerable to challenge. Mass automated approvals without documented individual review are structurally identical to mass automated denials — both are NULL.

The Burgess notice requirement does not only protect claimants. It protects the integrity of the process itself.

## IV. Application Two: Categorical Tariffs on UK Businesses

The United States government has threatened retaliatory tariffs against United Kingdom businesses in response to the UK's digital services tax. Those tariffs, if imposed, would be applied categorically — by sector, by country of origin, by product classification — without individual consideration of the specific circumstances of each affected business.

A UK business affected by categorical retaliatory tariffs faces the same structural failure as a US importer whose refund claim is bulk-processed. In both cases, institutional power is exercised over a specific person or entity without a specific human being having personally reviewed the specific facts of that specific situation.

### The binary test applied to categorical tariffs

| Scenario | Burgess test result |
|---|---|
| Tariff imposed on a specific business following documented individual review of that business's specific circumstances, trading history, and reliance interests | **SOVEREIGN** |
| Tariff imposed categorically by sector or country of origin, no named reviewer, no individual consideration of specific business circumstances | **NULL** — challengeable on Burgess notice grounds |
| Retaliatory tariff applied as a political instrument without individual adjudication of impact on specific businesses | **NULL** — no individual human review took place |

A UK business facing categorical retaliatory tariffs can invoke the same standard that a US importer invokes when challenging a bulk-processed refund denial. The question is the same in both jurisdictions: was a human being personally present at the decision point, reviewing the specific facts of this specific case, before the power was exercised?

If the answer is no — the finding is NULL. The challenge is available.

## VI. What This Means in Practice

### For US importers facing bulk-processed refund denials

Document the absence of individual review. Request in writing the name and role of the specific human being who reviewed your specific entry. If no individual reviewer can be named, the Burgess notice standard has not been met. A nonfrivolous allegation of entitlement, individually stated and individually unsupported by a named human reviewer, is the basis for a jurisdictional hearing under *Burgess v. MSPB*.

### For UK businesses facing categorical retaliatory tariffs

Document the categorical nature of the imposition. Request evidence that a specific human being reviewed the specific facts of your business's situation before the tariff was applied. If the imposition was categorical — by sector or country — no such reviewer exists. The NULL finding is available as a basis for challenge in any jurisdiction that recognises the individual review requirement, including under UK GDPR Article 22 and the DUAA 2025.

### For both governments

The Burgess notice standard does not prevent mass processing. It requires that mass processing be accompanied by individual human review of each case before a decision affecting that individual is made. Institutions that build processes capable of documenting individual review — with named reviewers, specific consideration of specific facts, and auditable records — are SOVEREIGN. Institutions that rely on automation, category, and bulk to substitute for that review are NULL, and their decisions are legally vulnerable on both sides of the Atlantic.
