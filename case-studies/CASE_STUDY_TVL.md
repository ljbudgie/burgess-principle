## Summary

Receiving letters that imply prosecution or a £1,000 fine can feel frightening, especially when you believe the letters should never have been sent in the first place. In this case, TV Licensing, operated by Capita on behalf of the BBC, escalated correspondence against an address without any identified human being first checking whether the occupier owned a television, watched live broadcasts, or used BBC iPlayer. The letters were driven by the absence of a licence record alone.

When the address was registered as **"No Licence Needed"**, the letters stopped. That matters because it shows a clear enforcement-presumption model: the system acted first, and only stopped when the false premise was corrected. Unlike the [Wave Utilities](CASE_STUDY_WAVE.md) case, this was not a debt reduced to £0.00 after human review. It was enforcement NULL to correspondence ceased because no licence was needed on the facts asserted by the complainant.

## Background

- **Complainant / applicant:** [Redacted].
- **Relevant account, document, or reference:** SAR reference POLSAR1321; "No Licence Needed" registration reference 4315773702.
- **Disability / adjustment context:** Accessible written communication requested as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.
- **What the institution did:** TV Licensing sent physical letters in escalating enforcement language without first checking whether the licence requirement applied to the specific address or whether reasonable adjustments were required.
- **Why the case matters:** It shows a pure enforcement-presumption model. Unlike a debt case where a balance can later be corrected, this case began from a premise that had never been individually verified at all.

The complainant's position was that no TV licence was required. Section 363 of the Communications Act 2003 criminalises unlicensed installation or use of television-receiving equipment for live broadcast reception; the absence of a licence on a database is not itself proof that those facts exist. The challenge also asserted data-rights requests under UK GDPR Articles 15, 21, and 22. BBC iPlayer use was part of the factual question asked, but the core point remained the same: TV Licensing had not individually checked whether the legal preconditions for enforcement messaging were present before acting.

## If This Is Happening To You

If you are getting letters like this, try not to panic. Many people feel anxious when a system uses formal language, prosecution wording, or large fines. This case shows that a calm, factual response can work.

### A gentle way to approach it

- **Start with the factual premise.** Ask whether anyone actually checked that a licence was needed for your address before the letters were sent.
- **Keep your wording simple.** You do not need to sound legalistic. The goal is to make the institution answer the human-review question clearly.
- **Ask for adjustments early if you need them.** If phone calls are difficult, say so and ask for email-only communication or another reasonable adjustment.
- **Keep everything together.** Save each letter, the envelope if useful, and any later confirmation that the address was recorded as "No Licence Needed."
- **Do not throw away the final confirmation.** The contrast between the threats and the later registration outcome is often the most important evidence.

### Recommended tools

- **Best starting template:** [General dispute with Burgess Principle](../templates/GENERAL_DISPUTE_WITH_BURGESS_PRINCIPLE.md)

- **Best Iris / sovereign workflow:** Ask Iris to separate the issues into factual premise, automated correspondence, and reasonable adjustment so the challenge stays precise.

### Evidence tips

- **Keep the full letter sequence in order.** It helps show the escalation pattern.
- **Pair the threats with the final outcome.** Preserve the final "No Licence Needed" confirmation together with the earlier letters.
- **Keep reference numbers visible.** POLSAR1321 and 4315773702 matter.
