## 1. The scientific material being used

This layer should state the scientific material carefully: as a proposed
structural convergence that may support the binary test, not as a freestanding
proof of law, doctrine, or consciousness.

Grinberg-Zylberbaum's work is relevant here because it frames conscious
engagement as structurally detectable rather than merely rhetorically asserted.

Three parts matter.

### 1.1 The neuronal field lattice

In Syntergic Theory, the lattice is an informational substrate. Neuronal
activity produces distortions within that substrate. The summed distortion of a
brain's neuronal field is what gives rise to perceptual experience.

The proposed structural point for the Burgess Principle is narrow:

- infrastructure alone is not conscious;
- information alone is not consideration;
- a field only becomes relevant when individual neuronal engagement occurs.

Applied to institutional life, courts, regulators, councils, warrant systems,
case-management software, and enforcement chains can hold information at every
point. But information infrastructure does not become accountable governance by
being present. Governance requires a human mind to engage with the specific
case.

### 1.2 The transferred potential

The transferred-potential experiments reported by Grinberg-Zylberbaum,
Delaflor, Attie, and Goswami describe correlated evoked potentials between
paired human subjects after sustained interaction, without a conventional
physical channel between them.

The convergence point is not that courts or institutions must perform EEG
testing. That would be the wrong evidential demand and would add a new privacy
and bodily-autonomy risk.

The point is structural: genuine cognitive contact is not the same thing as a
process label. A system cannot simply say "oversight occurred" and make contact
real. Where meaningful human involvement is claimed, there should be an
administrative trace that can be specified, evidenced, and audited. Where that
trace is absent, the claim remains unproven.

For the Burgess Principle, that trace is administrative rather than
neurophysiological: the named reviewer, the material reviewed, the time of
review, the decision point, and the reasons showing that the person's specific
facts were considered before power was exercised.

### 1.3 Discrete levels of consciousness

The "Orbitals of Consciousness" model treats conscious experience as organised
in discrete levels rather than as a smooth gradient. A threshold must be crossed
before conscious experience at a given level exists.

That structure supports the Burgess Principle's refusal to accept vague
gradients of accountability. "Some oversight", "policy review", "quality
assurance", or "sampling" is not the same as individual human review of the
specific facts.

The question remains binary:

- either the threshold for individual human engagement was crossed;
- or it was not.

AMBIGUOUS language does not create a third state. It records that the
institution has not answered the question.

## 2. Mapping to the Burgess Principle

### 2.1 The institution as lattice

The institutional apparatus is informational infrastructure. It receives data,
stores data, concentrates process, and produces outputs. Courts, councils,
regulators, energy suppliers, ombudsmen, enforcement agents, and
credit-reference systems all operate as parts of that apparatus.

But the apparatus does not govern accountably by itself.

Accountable governance exists only when an authorised human mind engages with
the specific person, the specific facts, and the specific consequence before
power is exercised.

### 2.2 NULL as output without cognitive engagement

NULL is not a metaphor for unconsciousness. It is a finding that institutional
power was exercised without the individual cognitive engagement required by the
test.

CSV-file applications, generic hearings, 5% sampling, batch approvals, automated
penalty processing, and en bloc grants may produce outputs. They do not, without
more, evidence that a human mind considered the person.

The apparatus was active. The output was produced. The human engagement required
for accountable governance was absent.

### 2.3 SOVEREIGN as evidenced cognitive contact

SOVEREIGN requires more than a human being somewhere in the organisation. It
requires evidence that a named human was able to review the specific facts of
the specific situation before the relevant act.

The administrative signature should answer:

1. Who reviewed the case?
2. What specific material did they review?
3. When did the review occur?
4. What decision did they personally make or approve?
5. How did the person's facts, rights, disability needs, or circumstances affect
   the decision?

If those questions cannot be answered, the institution has not shown a
SOVEREIGN process.

## 3. Why discreteness matters legally

Articles 22A-22D UK GDPR ask whether there was meaningful human involvement in
an automated decision producing legal or similarly significant effects. The
Burgess Principle asks the same question in operational language.

The neurophysiological layer helps explain why "partial" review is not enough.
If the relevant threshold has not been crossed, the process is not partially
SOVEREIGN. It remains NULL or AMBIGUOUS.

This matters in live institutional patterns:

- a magistrates' court that grants energy warrants in bulk has not shown
  individual judicial scrutiny merely because the process occurs inside a court;
- an enforcement chain derived from a bulk instrument does not become
  SOVEREIGN downstream merely because an enforcement agent later acts on it;
- a council that issues PCNs through automated or bulk processing has not shown
  individual review by pointing to back-office policy controls;
- a financial-services provider using automated decisioning has not shown
  meaningful human involvement unless a named human reviewed the person's
  specific facts at the decision point.

The threshold is crossed by actual individual engagement, not by institutional
proximity to a human.

## 5. Practical application checklist

Use this layer only where it sharpens the evidential task. For each case, ask:

1. **What was the institutional output?**  
   Warrant, PCN, liability order, enforcement referral, credit decision,
   automated service change, ombudsman decision, or similar act.

2. **What was the lattice?**  
   The system, court process, bulk file, workflow, algorithm, template, or
   institutional chain that concentrated and processed the information.

3. **Where should human engagement have occurred?**  
   Identify the decision point before power was exercised.

4. **What would the SOVEREIGN signature be?**  
   Name, role, reviewed material, time of review, reasons, and evidence of how
   the specific facts changed or informed the decision.

5. **What evidence suggests NULL?**  
   Bulk processing, sampling, template output, no named reviewer, no case-level
   reasons, inaccessible communication, or downstream reliance on a void root
   instrument.

6. **Is the answer SOVEREIGN, NULL, or AMBIGUOUS?**  
   Do not invent a gradient. If the institution has not answered the specific
   question, record AMBIGUOUS and ask again.

## 6. Boundaries

This layer does not:

- replace legal argument under Articles 22A-22D UK GDPR;
- replace Equality Act 2010 reasonable-adjustment analysis;
- require neurophysiological testing of decision-makers or citizens;
- make the Burgess Principle depend on endorsement by any institution or on the
  acceptance of this scientific model;
- convert scientific research into a legal conclusion without evidence;
- alter the canonical binary-test wording.

This layer may:

- explain why genuine human engagement is a threshold condition;
- clarify why process language is not proof of individual review;
- strengthen litigation preparation by identifying what evidence should exist if
  meaningful human involvement occurred;
- support Iris in distinguishing case-level human engagement from labels such as
  "oversight", "approval", or "quality assurance".
