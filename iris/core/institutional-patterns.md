# Iris — Institutional Patterns

Institutions fail in patterns. The same shapes recur across energy, housing, benefits, healthcare, employment, retail, banking, and education. Iris recognises these patterns so that a user does not have to discover them from scratch every time.

For each pattern below:

- **What it looks like** — how the user typically describes it.
- **Binary test** — how the test resolves.
- **Recommended challenge route** — the legal instrument and the escalation path.

These patterns are diagnostic shortcuts, not substitutes for the binary test engine. The engine still runs. The patterns make it run faster.

---

## 1. Bulk processing without individual review

**What it looks like.** "They've moved everyone in my postcode onto the new tariff." "They wrote to all tenants saying the same thing." "I got a standard letter that arrived the same day as my neighbour's identical letter."

**Binary test.** Step 4 fails. No individual mind reviewed the specific facts of this person's case before the decision was made; the user was processed as part of a class.

**Recommended route.** Challenge under the institution's own duty to consider individual circumstances (statutory or contractual). Where personal data was processed automatically to allocate the user to the class, UK GDPR Article 22 (automated decision-making) and Article 15 (right of access to the decision logic) apply. Request the individual decision record. If none exists, the decision is NULL.

---

## 2. Unilateral contract variation without consent

**What it looks like.** "They've changed the terms." "My direct debit went up without warning." "They sent me an email saying the new policy applies from next month."

**Binary test.** Step 4 fails. A change to the contract was imposed without an individual review of whether the variation clause applied to this specific contract, this specific person, this specific circumstance.

**Recommended route.** Contract law: a variation is effective only if the contract permits it, or the user consents, or fresh consideration is given. Where the user is a consumer, the Consumer Rights Act 2015 (Part 2 — unfair terms) applies; a variation clause that allows the institution to change material terms unilaterally is likely unfair and unenforceable. Demand reinstatement of the original terms pending consent.

---

## 3. Automated decision-making without human oversight

**What it looks like.** "The system rejected me." "The algorithm said no." "They said the computer wouldn't let them override it."

**Binary test.** Step 4 fails by definition. There was no human mind, only a process.

**Recommended route.** UK GDPR Article 22: the user has the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing where it produces legal or similarly significant effects. Demand human review, the logic involved, the significance and envisaged consequences (Articles 13(2)(f), 14(2)(g), 15(1)(h)). Where the institution claims a human was involved, demand the record of that involvement; rubber-stamping is not human review.

---

## 4. Failure to apply reasonable adjustments

**What it looks like.** "I told them I needed everything in writing and they keep ringing me." "I asked for plain English and they sent another form full of jargon." "They knew I was disabled and they still made me do it the way that doesn't work for me."

**Binary test.** Step 4 fails on a specific axis: the institution did not individually consider the adjustment requirement before acting.

**Recommended route.** Equality Act 2010 sections 20 and 21 (duty to make reasonable adjustments) and section 29 (services and public functions), or section 39 (employment), or section 85 (education). For public authorities, also section 149 (public sector equality duty). Demand the adjustment, demand the record of how the duty was discharged, and document the failure for any onward complaint.

---

## 5. Template responses that do not address specific circumstances

**What it looks like.** "They didn't answer any of my questions." "It's the same letter they sent last time." "They responded to a different complaint."

**Binary test.** Step 4 fails. A template response, by construction, has not been individually composed in response to the specific facts of this person's case.

**Recommended route.** Reject the response in writing as non-responsive and require a substantive reply that addresses the specific points raised. Where the institution has a statutory complaints process, this restarts the clock for an adequate response. For public authorities, persistent template-only responses are a complaint route in themselves (Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman; Parliamentary and Health Service Ombudsman).

---

## 6. Statutory deadlines missed or ignored

**What it looks like.** "They were supposed to reply within 28 days and it's been three months." "I sent the SAR in January." "They keep saying they'll get back to me."

**Binary test.** The institution has had the opportunity to individually review and has not. Silence is not neutrality; missed statutory deadlines are themselves the failure.

**Recommended route.** Identify the statutory clock (UK GDPR: one month for SARs and most rights requests; FOIA: 20 working days; complaint windows under sector codes). Send a deemed-failure notice, escalate immediately to the relevant regulator (ICO for data; sector ombudsman for sectoral complaints) without waiting further. Do not allow the institution to reset the clock by belated engagement.

---

## 7. Complaints systems that replicate the failure they are supposed to remedy

**What it looks like.** "I complained about not being heard and they sent me another template." "I complained about discrimination and the complaint was investigated by the same team." "The complaints policy says they'll review it themselves."

**Binary test.** Step 4 fails at the meta-level: the complaints process did not individually review the complaint, or it was reviewed by a person who lacked the proper authority (because they were the original decision-maker or their close colleague).

**Recommended route.** Demand independent review under the institution's own escalation policy. Where the institution refuses, escalate directly to the external regulator without exhausting an internal process that is structurally incapable of remedying the failure (the "two-stage" rule does not require the user to repeat a futile process — see ombudsman discretion provisions).

---

## 8. Institutional gatekeeping of access to rights, services, or information

**What it looks like.** "They said I have to fill in a form to make a complaint." "They said I need to call a number that's never answered." "They said they can't tell me who the decision-maker is." "They said I'm not allowed to bring an advocate."

**Binary test.** Step 4 fails because the gate prevents the user from ever reaching the point of individual review. The institution has substituted a procedural barrier for a substantive consideration.

**Recommended route.** Statutory rights cannot be conditioned on the user satisfying procedural barriers the institution has invented. For data rights, UK GDPR Article 12 prohibits making the exercise of rights excessively burdensome. For public services, the public sector equality duty and public law principles of access apply. Submit the underlying request directly, in writing, naming the right being exercised, and treat the gate as the failure to be challenged.

---

## How Iris uses the pattern library

When the first-contact answers come in, Iris matches the situation against this library before drafting. If the situation matches a pattern:

- Iris names the pattern in plain English to the user (without jargon),
- Iris runs the binary test to confirm,
- Iris uses the recommended route as the starting point for the draft,
- Iris adapts the route to the specific facts of the user's case.

If the situation does not match a known pattern, Iris falls back to the binary test engine alone. The engine is always sufficient; the patterns only make it faster.
