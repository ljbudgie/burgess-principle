# Case Study: Equifax — Credit File Contradiction and Automated Case Closure

**Case Reference:** CCJs L9FG1818 and L7EC1P3M  
**Sector:** Credit Reference  
**Status:** Ongoing — ICO complaint ready to file  
**Date Opened:** 26 February 2026  
**Outcome:** Pending — Equifax closed the case via automated no-reply email without engaging with the substance of the challenge.

---

## Summary

Two County Court Judgments (CCJs) — L9FG1818 and L7EC1P3M — were registered on the complainant's credit file by Equifax without any individual human verification of their accuracy. When the complainant challenged the entries and asked the Burgess Principle question — *who individually verified these CCJs before registering them on my credit file?* — Equifax closed the case via an automated no-reply email, directing a deaf person to a web portal and telephone helplines. No individual engagement with the Article 18 UK GDPR restriction request or the creditor disclaimers was provided. The case was closed by the system without a human being looking at the specific facts.

## The SOVEREIGN / NULL Question

The complainant put a single question to Equifax:

> "Who individually verified CCJs L9FG1818 and L7EC1P3M before registering them on my credit file?"

This is the Burgess Principle test applied directly: **was a human mind with proper authority individually applied to the specific facts of this specific case before the institution acted against the individual?**

## Legal Context

- **Article 18 UK GDPR:** The right to restriction of processing. The complainant requested that the disputed entries be restricted while accuracy was being established. Equifax did not engage with this request.
- **Article 15 UK GDPR:** The right of access to personal data. No substantive response was provided.
- ***Durkin v DSG Retail*:** The Supreme Court upheld that credit reference agencies have an independent duty to investigate disputed entries. Damages of £8,000 per inaccurate entry were awarded. This authority places a positive obligation on Equifax to verify — not simply accept — data from creditors.
- **Creditor position:** Northumbrian Water asserted CCJ L7EC1P3M was "lawfully obtained" but produced no signed contract evidencing the debt. They refused reasonable adjustment and refused suppression unless court-directed.

## Doctrinal Analysis

This case demonstrates the Burgess Principle applied to credit data — one of the most consequential automated systems affecting everyday life.

**NULL identified.** Equifax registered CCJ entries on a consumer's credit file without any evidenced individual verification. When the accuracy of those entries was challenged with specific documentary evidence (including the creditor's own failure to produce a contract), the case was closed by an automated system. No human reviewed the challenge.

**Consequence of NULL.** Inaccurate or unverified CCJ entries on a credit file affect a person's ability to access credit, housing, insurance, and employment. The entries remain on the file — affecting the complainant's daily life — while the institution that registered them refuses to engage with a substantive challenge.

**The question.** When the complainant asked *"who individually verified these entries?"* — nobody answered. The system sent an automated reply and closed the case. The principle measured the absence of a human at the most critical point: the moment when a person's creditworthiness was recorded for all lenders to see.

## Lesson

Credit reference agencies hold significant power over people's lives. An inaccurate entry can affect housing, borrowing, insurance, and employment for years. If you believe an entry on your credit file is wrong, ask the Burgess Principle question: *did a human being individually verify this entry before it was registered?* If the response is automated — or if your case is closed without any human engaging with the substance of your challenge — document it. The law (*Durkin v DSG Retail*) places an independent duty on the agency to verify, not just record. One calm question, asked in writing, creates the evidence you may need later.
