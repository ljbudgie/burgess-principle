# Case Study: Automated Passport Issuance — The SOVEREIGN/NULL Test Applied to Identity Documents

**Case Reference:** British Passport No. 133656141 (BURGESS, LEWIS JAMES)  
**Sector:** Identity and Travel Documents  
**Status:** Ongoing  
**Date Opened:** 30 March 2026  

---

## Summary

British Passport No. 133656141, issued 16 June 2022 to Lewis James Burgess by His Majesty's Passport Office (HMPO), was produced through centralised automated processing — including biometric capture, facial recognition matching, and database checks — without any named human decision-maker reviewing the applicant's individual circumstances. The passport's authority line reads "HMPO." No individual is named. No signature of a reviewing officer appears. No disclosed reasoning accompanies the determination. The applicant has bilateral sensorineural hearing loss and asserted email-only communication as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010, yet no documented mechanism exists within the issuance workflow for individually considering disability-related needs. This case study applies the Burgess Principle's binary SOVEREIGN/NULL test to the passport issuance process and finds it NULL at the point of issuance.

## Background

- **Subject:** Lewis James Burgess, born 24 June 1987, Lancaster, England.
- **Disability:** Bilateral sensorineural hearing loss. Email-only communication asserted as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.
- **Instrument:** British Passport No. 133656141, issued 16 June 2022 by HMPO.
- **Authority line on passport:** "HMPO" — no named individual, no signature of a reviewing officer, no disclosed reasoning.
- **Machine-readable zone (MRZ):** Encodes the holder's identity as an alphanumeric string processed by automated systems.
- **Biometric chip:** Contains facial recognition data captured and matched through automated processing.
- **Application process:** Submitted online or by post, processed through centralised automated systems (biometric capture, facial recognition matching, database checks), issued without any named human decision-maker reviewing the applicant's individual circumstances.
- **No documented Equality Act s.20 assessment** in the issuance workflow for hearing-impaired applicants.
- **Compulsory nature:** The passport is functionally compulsory for international travel. Refusal to accept the instrument results in exclusion from participation.

## The SOVEREIGN/NULL Question

The applicant puts a single question to HMPO:

> "Can you name the individual human being who personally reviewed my specific circumstances — including my disability — and who accepted personal liability for the accuracy of the identity determination before this passport was issued in my name?"

This is the Burgess Principle test applied directly: **was a human mind with proper authority individually applied to the specific facts of this specific case before the institution acted to define this individual's legal identity?**

## Analysis

### 1. Article 22 UK GDPR — Automated Decision-Making

Biometric passport processing involves automated decision-making that produces legal effects. The passport defines the holder's legal identity, enables international travel, and restricts it if refused. Under Article 22 of the UK GDPR, a data subject has the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing — including profiling — which produces legal effects concerning them or similarly significantly affects them.

The passport issuance process includes:

- **Facial recognition matching** — an automated biometric comparison with no disclosed human review of the match result for routine applications.
- **Database checks** — automated queries against government databases, producing pass/fail results processed without named human oversight.
- **Biometric chip encoding** — the applicant's facial recognition data is captured and encoded onto an RFID chip embedded in the passport.

At no documented stage does a named human decision-maker review the specific applicant's circumstances, apply individual judgment to the identity determination, or accept personal liability for the outcome. The identity determination — arguably the most significant legal classification any citizen carries — is produced by an automated pipeline.

Any citizen can file a subject access request (DSAR) with HMPO asking:

1. Whether automated decision-making (including profiling) was applied to their passport application.
2. The identity of any human decision-maker who reviewed their specific application.
3. The meaningful information about the logic involved in the automated processing.

If HMPO cannot name an individual, the Article 22 challenge is live.

### 2. Equality Act 2010 — Failure to Individually Consider Disability

The applicant has bilateral sensorineural hearing loss. Email-only communication has been asserted as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.

The passport issuance workflow processes every applicant identically, regardless of disability. No documented mechanism exists for:

- Identifying that an applicant has a hearing impairment at the point of application.
- Adjusting the communication method during processing (e.g., replacing telephone contact with email-only contact).
- Recording that a reasonable adjustment has been individually considered and applied at each stage.
- Ensuring that the applicant's specific needs are accommodated throughout — from application to biometric capture to issuance.

A deaf applicant's specific needs are not individually considered at any point in the automated pipeline. The system treats every applicant as interchangeable. This is the same structural failure identified in the Wave case study: an automated system that cannot distinguish between applicants with different needs because no human is present to ask the question.

### 3. Compulsory Acceptance — The Involuntary NULL Instrument

Unlike most institutional interactions, passport acceptance is functionally mandatory. A citizen cannot opt out of holding a passport without losing the ability to travel internationally. This distinguishes the passport from other institutional instruments:

- A utility bill can be challenged and the service switched.
- A debt referral can be disputed and withdrawn.
- A medical referral can be declined.

A passport cannot be refused without self-exclusion from international participation. The citizen must accept the instrument regardless of whether any human reviewed their application. This makes the SOVEREIGN/NULL question more acute: the individual has no practical alternative.

### 4. The Authority Line — No Named Individual

The passport names no individual decision-maker. Compare this to:

| Document | Authority Line | Named Individual |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Court judgment | "Before His Honour Judge [Name]" | Yes — the judge is named and personally accountable |
| Medical prescription | Signed by Dr [Name], GMC No. [Number] | Yes — the doctor is named and professionally liable |
| Legal opinion | "[Name], Solicitor/Barrister, [Firm]" | Yes — the lawyer is named and regulated |
| **British passport** | **"HMPO"** | **No — an institutional acronym, no individual named** |

The passport — arguably the most important identity document a citizen holds — is the only one issued by an acronym rather than a person. No individual accepts personal liability for the determination that the document accurately represents the identity of the holder.

## Historical Context

Centralised authorities issuing instruments over individuals without individual scrutiny is not a modern phenomenon. The colonial charter is one historical precedent: the First Virginia Charter of 1606, issued by James I under the Great Seal, projected Crown authority across the Atlantic without any individualised judicial scrutiny of the settlers' personal circumstances or rights. The Declaration of Independence in 1776 stands as a documented precedent for citizens collectively rejecting accumulated institutional authority exercised without individual consent — its catalogue of grievances describes, in 18th-century language, the consequences of centralised power acting on individuals without named human review. The same structural defect — centralised authority acting on individuals without a named human decision-maker applying individual judgment to the specific case — appears across centuries and across instrument types. The biometric passport is the modern expression of an ancient institutional pattern: an authority that processes individuals in bulk, issues instruments by institutional title rather than personal name, and accepts no individual liability for the determination. The Burgess Principle does not require historical comparison to function — the SOVEREIGN/NULL test stands on its own — but the pattern is observable across centuries of institutional architecture.

## Practical Steps for Any Citizen

Any citizen can apply the SOVEREIGN/NULL test to their own passport. The following steps require no legal training, no representation, and no confrontation.

### Step 1: File a Subject Access Request (DSAR) with HMPO

Under Article 15 of the UK GDPR, write to HMPO requesting:

> "Under Article 15 of the UK GDPR, I request the following information in relation to my passport application [reference/passport number]:
>
> 1. The identity of any named individual who personally reviewed my specific application before the passport was issued.
> 2. Whether automated decision-making, including profiling, was applied at any stage of processing my application.
> 3. Meaningful information about the logic involved in any automated decision-making applied to my application.
> 4. Whether any individualised assessment was conducted under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010 in relation to my application."

Send to: HMPO Data Protection Officer, HM Passport Office, Globe House, 89 Eccleston Square, London, SW1V 1PN. Or by email to the published HMPO data protection contact.

### Step 2: Assert Article 22 Rights

If the DSAR response confirms automated processing, write to HMPO asserting your rights under Article 22 of the UK GDPR:

> "Your response confirms that automated decision-making was applied to my passport application. Under Article 22(1) of the UK GDPR, I have the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing which produces legal effects concerning me. A passport defines my legal identity and enables or restricts international travel. I request that a named individual reviews my application, provides disclosed reasoning for the identity determination, and accepts personal liability for its accuracy."

### Step 3: Assert Equality Act Reasonable Adjustments (If Applicable)

If you have a disability, write to HMPO asserting your rights under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010:

> "I have [description of disability]. I require [specific adjustment, e.g., email-only communication] as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010. I request confirmation that this adjustment has been individually considered and applied at every stage of my passport application, and that this consideration is documented."

### Step 4: Document Everything

- Keep copies of all correspondence.
- Log dates, responses, and outcomes.
- If HMPO cannot name an individual, the SOVEREIGN/NULL finding is confirmed: your passport was issued without individual human review.

## Doctrinal Significance

This case study identifies a category of institutional instrument not previously documented under the Burgess Principle: **the compulsory NULL instrument**.

In the Wave case study, the automated enforcement referral was NULL — but the customer could challenge the debt, switch provider, or refuse the service. The NULL instrument was involuntary but not inescapable.

The passport is different. It is:

- **Compulsory** — no practical alternative exists for international travel.
- **Identity-defining** — it determines the holder's legal identity as recognised by other states.
- **Biometrically encoded** — the holder's facial recognition data is captured and stored on a chip without named human oversight.
- **Issued without individual review** — no named human decision-maker reviews the applicant's circumstances before issuance.
- **Unsigned by any individual** — the authority line reads "HMPO," not a named officer.

The citizen must accept this instrument or accept exclusion from international participation. There is no opt-out. There is no alternative provider. There is no appeal against the process itself — only against a refusal to issue. The compulsory NULL instrument is the strongest expression of the structural defect the Burgess Principle identifies: an institution acting on an individual without any named human applying individual judgment, where the individual has no practical choice but to accept the outcome.

The passport case does not require a specific complaint to demonstrate the defect. Every citizen's passport was issued the same way — without individual human review. That universality is the point.
