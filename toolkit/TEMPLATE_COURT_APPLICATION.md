# Court Application Guide

A practical guide explaining when and how to issue court proceedings. This is **guidance, not a fillable template** — court forms are issued by HM Courts and Tribunals Service and must be completed on their official forms.

**Important disclaimer:** This guide is provided for information only. It is not legal advice. Court proceedings have consequences, including costs. Consider seeking professional legal support — many solicitors offer a free initial consultation, and you may be eligible for legal aid or the help of a law centre. Citizens Advice can help you assess your options.

Based on the planned court proceedings following the E.ON Ombudsman ruling.

---

## When Court Proceedings Are Appropriate

Court action should generally be a last resort, after you have:

1. **Exhausted the complaints process** — you have complained to the institution and received a final response, or they have failed to respond within a reasonable time.
2. **Escalated to the ombudsman or regulator** — you have filed a complaint with the relevant ombudsman and received their ruling, or the ombudsman has declined to investigate.
3. **Documented everything** — you have a clear timeline, copies of all correspondence, and evidence of what was asked and what was or wasn't answered.

Court proceedings may be appropriate for:

- **Trespass claims** — where an enforcement agent entered your property without a valid warrant or lawful authority.
- **Equality Act claims** — where an institution failed to make reasonable adjustments under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.
- **Data protection claims** — where an institution failed to comply with a Subject Access Request or breached Article 22 UK GDPR (these can also be brought to the First-tier Tribunal).
- **Setting aside a County Court Judgment (CCJ)** — where a CCJ was obtained without proper service, without a valid contract, or based on automated processes without individual review.
- **Challenging a warrant** — where a warrant of entry was defective or issued without individual judicial consideration.

## The Claim Form Process

### County Court Claims (Form N1)

For most civil claims — trespass, Equality Act, breach of contract — you will use **Form N1**, the county court claim form.

**Where to get it:** [HM Courts and Tribunals Service — Form N1](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/form-n1-claim-form-cpr-part-7)

You can also issue claims online via [Money Claims Online](https://www.moneyclaims.service.gov.uk/) for claims up to £100,000.

**What to include:**

1. **Your details** (claimant) and the **defendant's details** (the institution's registered office address).
2. **Brief details of claim** — a short summary of what happened.
3. **Particulars of claim** — a more detailed statement of your case (see below).
4. **Value of claim** — the amount of compensation you are seeking, or "I expect to recover" followed by the relevant band.
5. **Statement of truth** — your signed declaration that the facts are true.

### Applications Within Existing Proceedings (Form N244)

If there are already court proceedings — for example, if a CCJ has been entered against you — you use **Form N244** to make an application within those proceedings. Common applications include:

- Setting aside a default judgment
- Applying for an injunction
- Requesting a stay of enforcement

**Where to get it:** [HM Courts and Tribunals Service — Form N244](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/form-n244-application-notice)

## What to Include in Particulars of Claim

Your particulars of claim should be a clear, chronological statement of the facts. Include:

1. **Who you are and who the defendant is.** One sentence each.
2. **The relevant background.** What account or relationship you have with the defendant.
3. **What happened.** A factual, dated account of the events. Use numbered paragraphs.
4. **What legal duty was breached.** State the specific statute or common law duty — e.g. "The defendant owed the claimant a duty to make reasonable adjustments under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010."
5. **How the breach caused you loss or harm.** Be specific — distress, financial loss, damage to property, damage to credit file.
6. **What you are claiming.** State the amount of compensation or the specific order you are seeking (e.g. "an order setting aside the CCJ dated...").

**Keep it factual.** The court is interested in what happened, what legal duty was breached, and what loss resulted. Do not include argument, emotion, or manifesto language.

## The Role of the Ombudsman Ruling

If you have an ombudsman ruling in your favour, it is a powerful foundation for court proceedings:

- It is an independent finding that the institution was at fault.
- It demonstrates that you exhausted the complaints process before issuing proceedings.
- It provides a baseline for the compensation the ombudsman considered appropriate — you can argue in court that the actual loss exceeded that amount.
- Courts generally expect claimants to have attempted alternative dispute resolution before issuing proceedings.

## Data Protection Claims

For claims based on breach of the UK GDPR or Data Protection Act 2018, you have the option of bringing your claim to the **First-tier Tribunal (Information Rights)** instead of, or in addition to, the county court.

The tribunal can order compliance with data protection obligations but cannot award compensation. For compensation, you need the county court.

You can also complain to the ICO, who can take enforcement action against the organisation.

## Practical Tips

1. **Keep a master timeline.** A single document listing every date, every letter, every response and non-response. This is the backbone of any court claim.

2. **Use your SAR responses as evidence.** If the institution couldn't name a human who reviewed your case, that is evidence for your claim.

3. **Be realistic about costs.** Even in the small claims track (up to £10,000), there are court fees, time, and stress involved. Make sure the claim is worth pursuing.

4. **Consider mediation.** The court may suggest or require mediation before a hearing. This can resolve disputes faster and cheaper than a full trial.

5. **Get the particulars right.** The particulars of claim are the most important document in your case. Consider having them reviewed by a solicitor or law centre, even if you represent yourself at the hearing.
