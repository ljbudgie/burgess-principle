# Case Study: Automated Passport Issuance — The SOVEREIGN/NULL Test Applied to Identity Documents

**Case Reference:** British Passport No. 133656141 (BURGESS, LEWIS JAMES)
**Sector:** Identity and Travel Documents
**Status:** Ongoing
**Date Opened:** 30 March 2026

---

## Summary

British Passport No. 133656141, issued 16 June 2022 to Lewis James Burgess by His Majesty's Passport Office (HMPO), was produced through centralised automated processing — including biometric capture, facial recognition matching, and database checks — without any named human decision-maker reviewing the applicant's individual circumstances. The passport's authority line reads "HMPO." No individual is named. No signature of a reviewing officer appears. No disclosed reasoning accompanies the identity determination. The applicant has bilateral sensorineural hearing loss, and no documented Equality Act section 20 assessment was conducted at any point in the issuance workflow. This case study applies the Burgess Principle's SOVEREIGN/NULL test to the most important identity document a citizen holds.

## Background

- **Applicant:** Lewis James Burgess, born 24 June 1987, Lancaster, England.
- **Disability:** Bilateral sensorineural hearing loss. Email-only communication asserted as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.
- **Instrument:** British Passport No. 133656141, issued 16 June 2022 by HMPO.
- **Authority line:** "HMPO" — no named individual, no signature of a reviewing officer, no disclosed reasoning.
- **Machine-readable zone (MRZ):** Encodes the holder's identity as an alphanumeric string processed by automated systems.
- **Biometric chip:** Contains facial recognition data captured and matched through automated processing.
- **Application process:** Submitted online or by post. The application entered a centralised automated pipeline: biometric data was captured, facial recognition matching was performed, database checks were executed, and the passport was issued — all without any named human decision-maker reviewing the applicant's individual circumstances.
- **Equality Act consideration:** No documented section 20 assessment in the issuance workflow for hearing-impaired applicants. The standard process assumes telephone helplines and in-person Post Office Check & Send services. No mechanism for reasonable adjustment — such as email-only communication — is documented at the point of issuance.
- **Compulsory acceptance:** A passport is functionally mandatory for international travel. Refusal to accept the instrument results in exclusion from participation. Unlike most institutional interactions, the citizen cannot opt out.

## The SOVEREIGN/NULL Question

The applicant puts a single question to HMPO:

> "Can you name the individual human being who personally reviewed my specific circumstances and who accepted personal liability for the accuracy of the identity determination before this passport was issued in my name?"

This is the Burgess Principle test applied directly: **was a human mind with proper authority individually applied to the specific facts of this specific case before the institution acted to define the individual's legal identity?**

The passport itself provides the answer. The authority line reads:

> **"HMPO"**

No individual name. No signature. No disclosed reasoning. No personal liability accepted.

**Finding: NULL.**

## Analysis

### 1. Article 22 UK GDPR — Automated Decision-Making

Biometric passport processing involves automated decision-making that produces legal effects. The passport defines the holder's legal identity and enables or restricts international travel. Under Article 22 of the UK GDPR, a data subject has the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing — including profiling — which produces legal effects or similarly significantly affects them.

The passport issuance process raises the Article 22 question directly:

- **Biometric capture:** Facial recognition data is captured from the applicant and stored on the passport's chip.
- **Automated matching:** The biometric data is matched against databases through automated processing.
- **Identity determination:** The system determines that the applicant is who they claim to be — a decision with profound legal consequences — without any documented meaningful human intervention in the specific case.
- **Legal effect:** The passport defines the holder's legal identity for the purposes of international travel, consular protection, and identity verification across institutions.

Was any human meaningfully involved in the identity determination for Passport No. 133656141? The authority line suggests not. The process, as designed, routes applications through automated systems. The question is whether any individual applied their mind to the specific facts of this specific application — or whether the passport was produced by a pipeline that processes every application identically, regardless of individual circumstances.

Any citizen can test this by filing a Data Subject Access Request (DSAR) with HMPO asking: (a) who specifically reviewed their application, (b) whether automated decision-making was applied, and (c) what meaningful human intervention, if any, occurred before the identity determination was made.

### 2. Equality Act 2010 — Failure of Reasonable Adjustment

The applicant has bilateral sensorineural hearing loss and has asserted email-only communication as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010.

The automated passport issuance pipeline processes every applicant identically. It does not include any documented mechanism for individualised consideration of disability at the point of issuance. The standard process assumes the applicant can use telephone helplines and attend in-person services. For a hearing-impaired applicant who requires email-only communication, no adjustment is documented.

This is not an allegation that HMPO refuses adjustments when asked. It is an observation that the automated pipeline — by design — does not individually consider whether an applicant has a disability that requires adjustment. The system cannot distinguish between an applicant who can use telephone services and one who cannot. Every applicant is processed through the same pipeline, and the passport is issued without any documented section 20 assessment.

The Burgess Principle does not require that every passport applicant receive a face-to-face interview. It requires that the system be capable of identifying when individual circumstances — including disability — require individual consideration, and that a named human being accepts responsibility for that determination.

### 3. Compulsory Acceptance of the Instrument

Most institutional interactions allow the citizen some degree of choice. A consumer can switch energy providers. A debtor can challenge a claim. A patient can seek a second opinion.

A passport is different. Without a valid passport, a citizen cannot travel internationally. The document is functionally compulsory for participation in modern civic and economic life. This makes the SOVEREIGN/NULL question more acute:

- The citizen cannot reject the instrument without losing the ability to travel.
- The citizen cannot obtain an alternative instrument from a competing provider.
- The citizen must accept the identity determination made by HMPO's automated systems — or accept exclusion.

When an instrument is compulsory, the standard for individual scrutiny should be higher, not lower. The citizen has no exit option. The least the issuing authority can provide is a named individual who reviewed the specific application and accepted liability for its accuracy.

### 4. The Authority Line — An Institutional Comparison

The passport — arguably the most important identity document a citizen holds — is issued under an acronym. Compare:

| Instrument | Authority Line |
|---|---|
| **Court judgment** | Names the judge who heard the case and applied their mind to the specific facts |
| **Medical prescription** | Names the doctor who examined the patient and accepted clinical liability |
| **Legal opinion** | Names the lawyer who reviewed the matter and accepted professional responsibility |
| **British passport** | "HMPO" — no named individual, no signature, no disclosed reasoning |

Every other instrument that materially affects a person's legal standing names the individual who made the determination. The passport — which defines the holder's legal identity — is the only one issued by an institution rather than a person.

## Historical Context

Centralised authorities issuing instruments over individuals without individual scrutiny is not a modern phenomenon. The First Virginia Charter of 1606, issued by James I from London, projected Crown authority across an ocean without any individualised review of the settlers' circumstances. The charter treated all settlers as interchangeable subjects of a blanket grant. The Declaration of Independence of 1776 stands as the most consequential practical rejection of accumulated institutional authority exercised without individual consent. The structural observation is straightforward: the same institutional architecture appears across centuries and across instrument types. A centralised body produces instruments that define individuals' legal status without a named human reviewing the specific case. The colonial charter, the blanket warrant, and the automated passport share the same defect. The medium changes — parchment, paper, polycarbonate — but the absence of a named, accountable human mind at the point of issuance does not. The Burgess Principle documents this pattern not as a historical grievance but as an observable architectural defect that persists in modern institutional design.

## Practical Steps for Any Citizen

The strength of this case study is its universal replicability. Every British passport holder can apply the same test to their own document.

### Step 1: Examine Your Passport

Look at the authority line. Does it name a specific, accountable human being — or does it read "HMPO"?

### Step 2: File a Data Subject Access Request (DSAR)

Write to HMPO (email preferred for audit trail) requesting:

> 1. The name of the individual who reviewed my specific passport application (reference: [your passport number]) before the passport was issued.
> 2. Confirmation of whether automated decision-making (including profiling) was applied in processing my application, and if so, what meaningful human intervention occurred.
> 3. The disclosed reasoning applied to my specific application — i.e., the basis on which my identity was determined.

HMPO is required to respond within one calendar month under Article 12 of the UK GDPR.

### Step 3: Assert Your Article 22 Rights

If HMPO confirms that automated decision-making was applied, write a separate letter asserting your rights under Article 22 of the UK GDPR:

> I assert my right under Article 22 of the UK GDPR not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing which produces legal effects concerning me or similarly significantly affects me. My passport defines my legal identity and enables or restricts my ability to travel internationally. I request confirmation that meaningful human intervention was applied to my specific application, or that the automated decision is reconsidered by a named individual.

### Step 4: Assert Equality Act Reasonable Adjustments (If Applicable)

If you have a disability, write to HMPO asserting your reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010:

> I have [describe disability]. I require [describe adjustment, e.g., email-only communication] as a reasonable adjustment under section 20 of the Equality Act 2010. Please confirm whether this adjustment was individually considered at the point of my passport application and issuance, and if not, what mechanism exists for such consideration.

### Step 5: Document Everything

- Save every email, letter, and reference number.
- Note dates and record names — or the absence of names.
- Institutional silence is itself evidence that no individual review occurred.
- The passport itself is the primary evidence: if the authority line reads "HMPO" and no individual is named, the instrument answers its own SOVEREIGN/NULL test.

## Biometric NULL Propagation via DSAR Identity Verification

The DSAR itself creates a secondary SOVEREIGN/NULL test — a **propagation layer** — through the identity verification process that precedes disclosure.

### The Propagation Paradox

To respond to a DSAR, the data controller must verify the identity of the requester. For HMPO, this means verifying that the person requesting the data is the same person to whom Passport No. 133656141 was issued.

But the identity determination that produced Passport No. 133656141 was itself NULL — no named individual reviewed it, no disclosed reasoning accompanied it, no personal liability was accepted for its accuracy. The DSAR requester is now asking the institution to verify their identity against a record whose own identity determination was made without individual human scrutiny.

This creates a **NULL propagation chain**:

1. **Original identity determination (NULL):** The passport was issued through automated processing. No named human verified that the biometric data, the photograph, and the applicant's claimed identity were accurate for this specific individual.
2. **DSAR identity verification (NULL?):** To verify the requester's identity, HMPO will match the requester's provided data against the record — a record whose accuracy was never individually verified by a named human being.
3. **Biometric loop:** If HMPO uses biometric matching to verify the DSAR requester's identity — comparing a submitted photograph against the facial recognition template stored on the chip — the institution is verifying identity against biometric data that was itself captured, processed, and matched without individual human review.

### The SOVEREIGN/NULL Question at the Verification Layer

The question becomes: **who personally verified that the biometric template stored on the chip of Passport No. 133656141 accurately represents Lewis James Burgess?**

If the answer is "no one — the biometric capture and matching were automated," then the DSAR identity verification inherits the same NULL defect as the original issuance. The institution is verifying identity against an unverified baseline.

This is not a theoretical problem. It is an observable architectural feature: the same automated pipeline that cannot provide a named individual at the point of issuance also cannot provide a named individual at the point of identity verification for data access. The NULL propagates.

### Doctrinal Implication

The biometric NULL propagation layer demonstrates that automated identity systems do not merely fail the SOVEREIGN/NULL test at a single point — they propagate the failure through every subsequent interaction that relies on the original determination. Each layer of the system inherits the defect of the layer beneath it. No amount of automated cross-referencing corrects the original absence of individual human scrutiny.

The DSAR is therefore not only a mechanism for obtaining evidence — it is itself a test that reveals the propagation depth of the NULL finding.

## Doctrinal Significance

This case adds a critical category to the Burgess Principle framework: **the compulsory NULL instrument**.

In previous case studies — such as [Wave Utilities](CASE_STUDY_WAVE.md) — the NULL finding applied to enforcement actions that the citizen could challenge, negotiate, or ultimately resolve through individual human review. The Wave case demonstrated the complete SOVEREIGN/NULL cycle: NULL at the point of automated referral, SOVEREIGN at the point of human resolution.

The passport presents a different problem. The citizen cannot challenge, negotiate, or opt out of the instrument. The passport is compulsory for international travel. There is no alternative provider. There is no appeals process that results in a named individual signing the document. The NULL finding at the point of issuance is not a defect that can be corrected through the institution's existing complaints process — it is a structural feature of the system's design.

This makes the passport the paradigm case for compulsory NULL instruments: documents that every citizen must accept, issued without any named individual accepting liability, through a process that cannot distinguish between applicants regardless of their individual circumstances.

The Article 22 UK GDPR challenge and the Equality Act section 20 assertion provide two evidence-based legal avenues for any citizen to test the system. Neither requires legal representation. Both produce documented evidence. The DSAR is the mechanism; the passport is the evidence; the SOVEREIGN/NULL test is the question.
