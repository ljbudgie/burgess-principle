# Warrant-Specific Subject Access Request

A forensic SAR template specifically for challenging warrants of entry. Based on the actual DSAR methodology used in the E.ON case (Warrant RE99022) and designed to surface whether a magistrate individually reviewed the application or whether the warrant was batch-processed.

This template targets the key vulnerability in the bulk warrant system: the requirement under the Rights of Entry (Gas and Electricity Boards) Act 1954, section 2(1), that a warrant must be issued by a justice of the peace "under his hand" — meaning individual judicial consideration, not rubber-stamping.

Send this by email where possible. Record the date — the organisation has **30 calendar days** to respond.

---

## Template

```
Subject: Subject Access Request — Warrant [WARRANT REFERENCE NUMBER]

Dear Data Protection Officer,

[INSTITUTION NAME]

I am writing to make a Subject Access Request under Article 15 of the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) and the Data Protection Act 2018.

This request relates specifically to the warrant of entry executed or
obtained in relation to my property at [PROPERTY ADDRESS], reference
[WARRANT REFERENCE NUMBER], dated [WARRANT DATE].

Please provide the following information:

1. PERSONAL DATA
   A copy of all personal data you hold about me, in any format, including
   account records, correspondence, internal notes, call recordings, and
   file notes.

2. THE WARRANT
   (a) A full copy of the warrant document itself
   (b) A copy of the application made to the court for the warrant
   (c) The name of the magistrate or justice of the peace who reviewed
       and signed the warrant application
   (d) Confirmation of whether the warrant application was individually
       heard by a magistrate, or whether it was part of a batch of
       applications processed in a single session
   (e) If batch-processed, the total number of warrant applications
       processed in the same session
   (f) Any metadata associated with the warrant document, including
       digital signatures, timestamps, creation dates, and authoring
       software

3. ENFORCEMENT AGENT
   (a) The instruction or referral document sent to the enforcement agent
       authorising the visit to my property
   (b) The enforcement agent's report or notes from the visit
   (c) Any bodycam or recording footage from the visit on [DATE OF ENTRY]
   (d) The name of the individual within your organisation who authorised
       the referral to the enforcement agent

4. VULNERABILITY AND ACCOUNT FLAGS
   (a) Any vulnerability flags, Priority Services Register (PSR) entries,
       or disability-related notes on my account at the time the warrant
       application was made
   (b) Confirmation of whether these flags were considered as part of the
       warrant application process

5. AUTOMATED DECISION-MAKING
   Under Article 22 UK GDPR, please confirm whether any decision in the
   chain — from account referral to warrant application to enforcement
   instruction — was made by an automated system without meaningful human
   intervention.

   If automated processing was used at any stage, please provide:
   (a) The logic involved in the automated processing (Article 15(1)(h))
   (b) The significance and envisaged consequences of that processing
   (c) The identity of any human who meaningfully reviewed my specific
       case before the decision was made at each stage

6. HUMAN REVIEW
   Please provide the name and role of any individual who personally
   reviewed my specific account before:
   (a) The decision was made to apply for a warrant
   (b) The warrant application was submitted to the court
   (c) The enforcement agent was instructed to attend my property

7. THIRD-PARTY SHARING
   Please provide details of any third parties with whom my personal data
   has been shared in connection with this warrant, including:
   (a) The identity of each third party
   (b) The categories of data shared
   (c) The lawful basis relied upon for each disclosure

8. RETENTION PERIODS
   Please confirm the retention period for each category of personal data
   you hold about me, or the criteria used to determine the retention
   period.

IDENTITY VERIFICATION
I confirm my identity as follows:
- Full name: [YOUR NAME]
- Date of birth: [YOUR DOB]
- Address: [YOUR ADDRESS]
- Email: [YOUR EMAIL]
- Account/Reference: [ACCOUNT/REFERENCE NUMBER]

I am happy to provide further identification if reasonably required.

DEADLINE
Under Article 12(3) UK GDPR, you are required to respond to this request
within 30 calendar days of receipt. If you are unable to comply within
that period, you must inform me of the reason for the delay and the
expected date of response.

Yours faithfully,

[YOUR NAME]
[YOUR ADDRESS]
[YOUR EMAIL]
```

## Why This Template Works

The Rights of Entry (Gas and Electricity Boards) Act 1954, section 2(1), requires that a warrant of entry be issued by a justice of the peace "under his hand." This means a magistrate must individually consider the specific application — not sign off on a batch of hundreds in a single sitting.

The questions in this template are designed to establish whether that individual consideration took place. If the institution cannot demonstrate that a magistrate individually reviewed your case, the warrant may be legally defective.

---

## Context: The E.ON Precedent

In the E.ON case (Warrant RE99022), metadata analysis revealed that the warrant document was a CSV-generated template with no evidence of individual judicial scrutiny. Andrew Fletcher, E.ON's representative, acknowledged in correspondence that the warrant was transmitted digitally and processed as part of a bulk application. This template is based on the SAR methodology that surfaced those facts.

## After Sending

- Record the date you sent the request.
- If they do not respond within 30 days, see the [Ombudsman/Regulator Complaint](TEMPLATE_OMBUDSMAN_REFERRAL.md) template.
- If they provide the warrant but cannot answer the questions about individual judicial consideration, that non-answer is itself significant evidence.
- Keep every response — it forms part of your evidential record.
