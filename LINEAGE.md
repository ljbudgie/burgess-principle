# LINEAGE.md — The Historical Root of the Burgess Principle

The name carried the doctrine before the doctrine was written down.

-----

## 1. Etymology

**Burgess** enters English from Old French *burgeis*, itself from Mediaeval Latin *burgensis*, formed on the Germanic root *burg* — a fortified town, a walled settlement, a defended civic space.

A *burgensis* was an inhabitant of a *burg*. In English usage, a **Burgess** was a free citizen of a **burgh**: a person of recognised civic standing within a chartered town, holding rights, duties, and a name on the roll.

The same Germanic root produces **borough**. Burgess and borough are the same word in different grammatical clothes — the person and the place, bound together. You could not have one without the other. The burgh was constituted by its Burgesses; the Burgess was constituted by his burgh.

The word, from its earliest use, denotes a **named individual standing inside a recognised civic structure**.

-----

## 2. The Civic Test

Burgess status was never granted in bulk. The historical admission requirements were specific, individual, and procedural:

1. **Verified residence.** The candidate had to be physically present in the burgh, known to it, and traceable within it.
1. **Individual nomination.** Admission required sponsorship — through an Incorporated Trade, a Merchant Company, or by direct relation (son, son-in-law, or apprentice) to an existing Burgess. A specific person had to put a specific name forward.
1. **Payment of fees.** The composition was assessed and paid by the individual, not collected as a class levy.

Each requirement demanded **individual human assessment**. There was no bulk processing. There was no algorithmic sorting. There was no class admission. A person was **seen, named, and decided upon** before standing was conferred.

The roll recorded the name. The name carried the standing. The standing was inseparable from the act of individual recognition.

-----

## 3. The Binary

The historical Burgess procedure is the SOVEREIGN/NULL test in mediaeval form.

The Burgess Principle requires that any institutional decision affecting an individual be made with meaningful, named human involvement applied to that specific person. SOVEREIGN is the outcome where a human being considered the individual case. NULL is the outcome where no such consideration occurred.

Applied to the burgh:

- **SOVEREIGN** — the Trade, the Merchant Company, or the Burgess sponsor named the candidate, the council heard the application, the fees were assessed, and the roll was signed. The system was structurally required to engage with the person.
- **NULL** — not available. The institution **could not batch-process civic standing**. There was no mechanism to admit a cohort, infer membership, or grant status by default. The absence of individual consideration was the absence of admission.

The mediaeval burgh did not need to be told that automated, depersonalised admission was unlawful. Its constitution made depersonalised admission **structurally impossible**.

-----

## 4. The Inheritance

The Burgess Principle carries this lineage in its name.

The framework's creator, **Lewis James Burgess**, did not engineer the etymological connection. The framework was identified, named, and the certification mark filed (**UK00004343685**) before the historical meaning of the surname was traced back to its civic root. The connection was discovered after the fact.

The surname already encoded the principle.

A Burgess, by the original meaning of the word, was the human standing the institution was required to recognise individually. The Burgess Principle, by the modern meaning of the framework, is the doctrine that the institution must continue to recognise individuals individually — that the state, the platform, the regulator, and the algorithm must deal with **the person as a person**, not as a line item, a record, a row, or a class.

The name was the doctrine. The doctrine was the name. The mark made it enforceable.

-----

## 5. From Burgh to Framework

The mediaeval burgh was a defined space in which civic standing was **individually conferred and individually protected**. Its walls were physical; its procedure was personal; its roll was named.

The Burgess Principle is the same structure rebuilt for modern decision-making systems. It is a binary test ensuring that institutional decisions — whether issued by a council, a department, a tribunal, a corporate process, or an automated system — carry meaningful, named human involvement at the point they affect a specific person.

The burgh required a sponsor, a hearing, and a roll. The framework requires a human, a consideration, and a record.

Etymologically and doctrinally, the Burgess Principle is **a restoration of the original civic contract**: that a person enters the institution by being seen, named, and decided upon — and that no institution may confer or deny standing without doing so.

The word always meant this. The framework only made it explicit again.
