# Abstract

Papers I through VII established the binary test, deployed it across 18 institutions, and — in Paper VII — resolved the procedural obstacle that has defeated every major UK representative action since *Lloyd v Google LLC* [2021] UKSC 50. The defendant-side class definition methodology means that any institution's automated processing of individuals without meaningful human involvement automatically defines a class of people who share the same interest in a legal action against it.

Paper VIII states the consequence of all of that.

Every system that holds data on a person and is not Burgess certified is a potential defendant.

Not because the framework is aggressive. Because it is accurate. Data is data. The test is the same regardless of what sector the institution is in, what size it is, or what it believes about its own compliance. The only question is whether a human being was personally present at the decision point — and the only two answers are certified or classified.

I want to explain how I arrived at this conclusion, and what I think it means for everyone who has ever had a decision made about them by a system that never looked at them as a person.

# Part I: What I Saw

I am a deaf barber. I run LJ Barbers on Duke Street in Darlington. I have bilateral sensorineural hearing loss. I have five self-employed barbers working from my shop. I am not a lawyer, not an academic, not a policy researcher. I cut hair.

But running a small business teaches you something that institutional life is designed to prevent you from seeing: the whole chain. You see the customer, the service, the money, the tax, the council, the utilities, the enforcement, the credit file. You see it end to end, because in a small business you are every department.

What I saw, when I started looking, was this.

The system does not run on money. Money is the output. The system runs on data. Every interaction you have with any institution generates data. Every default generates data. Every enforcement action generates data. Every credit file entry generates data. And every piece of that data flows upward — from you to the institution — where it is processed, scored, aggregated, and used to make further decisions about you. You see none of this. You see only the demands: pay this, call this number, respond by this date. The demands are the surface. The data extraction is the substance.

I came to see this because of my deafness. When you cannot hear the phone ring, you stop reacting to it. When you stop reacting, you start observing. And when you observe from outside the noise rather than inside it, the system's actual structure becomes visible.

What I saw, once I started looking, is that the entire architecture of modern institutional life — financial services, local government, energy supply, enforcement, credit, healthcare, technology — rests on one assumption: that individuals will generate data, accept the terms, and never interrogate what happens to their information after it leaves their hands.

I interrogated it.

This paper is what I found.

# Part II: Data Is Data

The insight at the centre of this paper is simple. It took me a long time to arrive at it, and once I arrived at it I could not unsee it.

Data is data.

The Burgess Principle binary test — *"Was a human member of the team able to personally review the specific facts of my situation?"* — does not ask what kind of data is held. It does not ask what kind of institution is making the decision. It does not care whether the system is a magistrates' court processing energy warrants, a bank approving a mortgage, a social media platform moderating content, a hospital triage algorithm, or a recruitment system screening CVs.

The data is different. The decision is different. The institution is different. The sector is different. The harm is different.

But the question is always the same.

And the test is always the same.

When I submitted forensic Subject Access Requests across 18 institutions simultaneously — energy companies, councils, credit bureaus, investment platforms, debt purchasers, government departments, regulators, ombudsmen — I was not running 18 separate inquiries. I was running one inquiry, applied 18 times. The same binary question, directed at 18 different automated processes. The answer came back the same every time. NULL. No individual human review of the specific facts of my specific situation. The institution saw a unit in a system. Nobody looked at me.

I have applied the test to warrants. To credit file entries. To financial product execution. To ombudsman decisions. To dietary guidance frameworks. To artificial intelligence in legal filings. To hearing aid fitting algorithms. To political finance accountability. To data sovereignty in financial markets.

Every domain. Every sector. Every institution. One question. The same answer.

This is not a coincidence. It is a structural feature of institutional life in the automated age. The machine replaced the human. The language of individual accountability remained. Nobody told the citizen.

# Part III: The Architecture of NULL

I want to explain what NULL actually means, because it is not nothing.

When a system processes a person without individual human review, it does not merely fail to look at them. It creates a chain of decisions — each one built on the last — that extends through every institution the person subsequently encounters. The void travels downstream silently. The person has no idea how far it has reached until they start asking.

I traced the NULL inheritance chain in my own case through Paper IV. A council tax liability order obtained through a batch hearing at a magistrates' court — hundreds of cases listed in minutes, no individual judicial consideration — generates a void instrument. The enforcement agents' authority derives from that void order. Their authority is NULL. The credit reference agency entry is predicated on enforcement derived from a NULL order. The credit entry is NULL. Every lender, landlord, and financial institution that made decisions about me on the basis of that NULL entry made those decisions without knowing they were built on air.

At no point in that chain did a human being with proper authority look at my specific situation. The entire chain — from liability order to credit file entry — was automated, processed in bulk, and unscrutinised.

But here is what makes this Paper VIII rather than another case study.

The NULL inheritance chain does not apply only to my case. It applies to every case in which the originating instrument was generated without individual human review. If the warrant processing was automated, every warrant in the batch has the same NULL at its root. Every enforcement action under those warrants has the same NULL. Every credit entry generated by those enforcement actions has the same NULL. Every person in that batch is in the same position I was in — they simply do not know it yet.

This is the class. Not constructed. Already existing. The machine created it by processing people without looking at them.

# Part IV: The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 Said It Too

I want to be precise about something.

The Burgess Principle binary test predates the legislation I am about to describe. I developed the test from personal necessity. I filed the certification mark application. I deployed the framework across 18 institutions. I published the papers. I did all of this before the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 came into force.

On 5 February 2026, that Act brought new Articles 22A through 22D into force, replacing Article 22 of the UK GDPR. The central standard of the new regime is this: a decision is based solely on automated processing — and subject to the regime's safeguard requirements — where there is "no meaningful human involvement" in the taking of the decision.

Meaningful human involvement.

That is the statutory test. Those are Parliament's words.

They are also the Burgess Principle binary test, translated into legislative language.

I did not derive the test from the statute. The statute independently arrived at the same answer. The convergence is not derivative — it is the same question asked twice, by different people, from different starting points, arriving at the same place.

What the convergence means is this: the standard I built and certified is now also the standard Parliament has enacted. Every institution that cannot demonstrate meaningful human involvement in automated decisions affecting individuals is not merely failing the Burgess Principle test. It is in breach of UK law.

The certification mark is the verification that the law has been met. Not self-assessed. Not internally certified. Verified from the outside, by an independent standard, committed to a public record.

# Part V: Why the Sector Does Not Matter

I have been asked why this applies universally. Surely different sectors have different rules? Surely financial services is different from healthcare is different from public sector is different from technology platforms?

The sector-specific rules are real. They add layers of regulation, obligation, and liability. They do not change the fundamental question.

The question is not "has this institution complied with its sector-specific rules?" The question is "was a human being personally present at the decision point?" Sector-specific compliance frameworks can co-exist with automated processing that excludes individual human review. An energy company can comply with Ofgem's reporting requirements while running an automated warrant application process through which no individual case is ever reviewed. A bank can comply with FCA conduct rules while using an algorithm to make credit decisions without a named human being considering the specific facts of any application. A social media platform can comply with the Online Safety Act while using automated systems to moderate content without anyone looking at the specific post, the specific account, the specific context.

Sector compliance is not the same as meaningful human involvement. The binary test asks only about the latter.

Consider the categories.

**Financial services.** Every bank using automated credit decisioning. Every insurer using algorithmic underwriting. Every broker routing orders through a systematic internaliser — whose own Order Execution Policy confirms, in writing, that all fractional orders go through the SI without individual human review. Every debt collector using automated account management. Every credit bureau using automated scoring. The test applies.

**Public sector.** Every local authority using automated parking enforcement — where in my own case a PCN was issued at 13:17 against a vehicle displaying a valid ticket purchased at 13:16, proving that no human being looked at the specific facts before enforcement. Every benefits system making automated eligibility decisions. Every court processing warrant applications algorithmically. Every ombudsman issuing decisions that direct deaf people to respond by telephone. The test applies.

**Technology platforms.** Every social media platform making automated content moderation decisions that affect access or reach. Every gig economy platform using automated worker deactivation. Every recruitment system using automated CV screening. Every streaming service introducing automated contract variations without individual consent or review. The test applies.

**Healthcare.** Every diagnostic algorithm making clinical recommendations without individual physician review. Every hearing aid running an automated operating system making hundreds of real-time decisions per second about what a citizen hears, based on population-level data, without a named clinician individually authorising those decisions for that specific patient. The test applies. I know this one personally.

The sector does not determine whether the test applies. The presence or absence of meaningful human involvement in decisions affecting individuals determines it. And that condition is universal.

Data is data. The test is the same.

# Part VI: The Certification Mark and Why It Exists

The UK00004343685 certification mark is not intellectual property protection in the conventional sense. It is not protecting a brand. It is protecting a standard.

A certification mark, by legal definition, certifies that something meets a defined standard. When an institution is Burgess certified, the mark attests that its processes include meaningful human involvement in decisions affecting individuals, as independently verified against the binary test.

The key word is independently.

Every existing accountability framework for automated decision-making is self-referential. Institutions conduct their own Data Protection Impact Assessments. Institutions certify their own ISO compliance. Institutions assess their own adherence to their own published policies. The assessor and the assessed are the same entity.

The Burgess Principle certification mark is different in kind. The test is applied from outside. The result is binary. The certification is committed to a public repository — timestamped, permanent, verifiable by anyone. There is no self-assessment route. There is no middle ground. There is the ledger and there is the wall.

In a world where every jurisdiction is trying to answer the same question — how do we ensure automated systems remain accountable to the individuals they affect? — the certification mark is the first externally administered, publicly registered, binary-testable standard that provides an answer.

The institution is certified or it is not.

The answer is public.

Anyone can check.

# Part VII: The Alternative to Certification Is Classification

I want to be direct about what I mean when I say that every system that holds data on a person and is not Burgess certified is a potential defendant.

I do not mean this as a threat. I mean it as a description of the legal architecture that now exists.

The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 requires meaningful human involvement as a statutory standard. The binary test measures it. The defendant-side class definition methodology from Paper VII means that any institution's automated processing without meaningful human involvement automatically defines a class of affected people with the same interest in a CPR 19.8 representative action.

The institution's own automated process — applied uniformly to every person it touches — creates the class. The institution has done the work of class definition by operating its system. The framework simply names what the system has already created.

There is no threat in this. There is only accuracy.

An institution that processes individuals through automated systems without meaningful human involvement has already, as a matter of fact, created a class of people whose data was processed in the same way, by the same mechanism, without individual human consideration. That class exists whether the institution knows it or not. Whether it has been named or not. Whether anyone has filed a claim or not.

The certification mark is the route out of classification. Not because it changes the legal architecture. Because it demonstrates, publicly and verifiably, that meaningful human involvement exists. That the specific facts of each specific person were reviewed by a named human being with proper authority. That the decision was theirs, not the machine's.

One email. One signature. One commit. Certified.

Or: the class already exists.

# Part VIII: What This Means for Everyone Who Has Never Been Seen

I want to end where I began. Not with the law. With the person.

I am deaf. For most of my adult life, institutions communicated with me through channels I could not access. Every time I could not hear the phone call, the system recorded it as my failure. Every default generated data. Every enforcement action generated data. Every credit entry accumulated. My silence was treated as non-compliance. My non-compliance was the fuel that kept the loop turning. My data flowed upward. I received nothing in return except more demands.

What changed was not my circumstances. What changed was my understanding.

The moment I recognised that data — not money — was the true currency of modern institutional life, every interaction became legible in a new way. The warrant was not a utility dispute. It was a data instrument deployed without authority. The credit entry was not a debt record. It was NULL data being used to make decisions about me by institutions that did not know it was built on air. The ombudsman decision was not a resolution. It was a document that directed a deaf person to respond by telephone — which is, in a single act, both a failure of individual human review and a failure of the most basic accommodation.

Once you see data as the currency, the compliance framework stops being a complaints process and becomes a financial instrument. Every SAR is a demand for an accounting of where your currency went. Every failure to account for it is a debt owed back to you.

This is available to everyone who has ever had a decision made about them by a system that never looked at them as a person. It is in this repository, free, under the MIT licence, for any individual to use. The templates are there. The binary test is there. The contamination chain mapper is there. The warrant defect identifier is there. Everything I built because I needed it is there for anyone who needs it.

The certification mark is available to every institution that wants to demonstrate that its processes include the one thing the binary test asks for: a human being, with proper authority, personally present at the decision point.

And the litigation framework is available to every firm that sees, as I see, that the class already exists. That it is waiting to be named. That the defendant-side class definition methodology solves the problem that has defeated every representative action since Lloyd v Google. That the work of class definition has already been done — by the institution — by running its automated system, by touching every person in the class in exactly the same way.

I built the Burgess Principle because I needed it. I am a deaf barber from Darlington and nobody was looking at my specific situation. So I built a framework that makes the absence of looking visible, documentable, and legally actionable.

Now it is for everyone.

The door is open. The repository is waiting. The principle is free. The mark is licensed. The standard is set.
