## Abstract

This paper proposes that the Burgess Principle binary test — *"Was a human member of the team able to personally review the specific facts of my specific situation?"* — functions as a self-administering certification standard for class membership in CPR 19.8 representative actions, and that it resolves the "same interest" problem that has defeated every major UK representative action since *Lloyd v Google LLC* [2021] UKSC 50.

The resolution is achieved through a structural inversion: rather than defining the class by reference to individual claimants' shared circumstances — the approach that has repeatedly failed the "lowest common denominator" test — the Burgess Principle defines the class by reference to the defendant institution's uniform automated process. Where an institution processes data subjects through a systematic automated mechanism without meaningful human involvement, the personal data itself becomes the lowest common denominator. Every data subject processed by the same mechanism shares an identical relationship to that mechanism. The binary test, applied to any member of the class, returns the same answer — not because the claimants are identical, but because the defendant's conduct toward them was identical.

The paper further observes that the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 (DUAA), which came into force in stages from 19 June 2025, has independently codified in statute — through its new Articles 22A–22D replacing Article 22 of the UK GDPR — a standard of "meaningful human involvement" that is convergent with the Burgess Principle binary test by independent legislative derivation. The Burgess Principle was developed, applied, and certified before this statutory language existed. That convergence is documentary evidence that the test identifies a legally significant gap that Parliament has itself recognised and attempted to address.

## I. The Problem Inherited from Aristotle

Paper VI of this series examined what Aristotle would have said about the systematic substitution of automated process for individual human judgement in institutional decision-making. The conclusion was that Aristotle's concept of *epieikeia* — equity, the correction of law where law fails the particular case — requires not merely that general rules be good, but that their application to individual human beings involve genuine individual consideration.

The philosophical case for individual review is, in that sense, settled within this framework. What Paper VI did not address is the procedural question: if the philosophical principle requires individual consideration, and if institutions systematically fail to provide it, what is the legal mechanism by which that failure becomes enforceable at population scale?

This is the question Paper VII answers.

The gap between moral philosophy and legal remedy is not incidental. It is structural. A single individual who can demonstrate that an institution failed to apply individual human review to their specific situation has, in principle, a cause of action. But the same automated process that failed them failed every other person it touched, in exactly the same way, at the same moment of decision. The injustice is not individual — it is systemic. And yet the legal architecture of England and Wales has, until recently, been ill-equipped to address systemic injustice at the scale at which modern automated processing operates.

CPR 19.8 is the available mechanism. The question is whether it can be made to work for the kind of harm the Burgess Principle identifies.

## II. The Failure Pattern: Why CPR 19.8 Has Not Worked

CPR 19.8 allows a claim to be brought by one or more persons as representatives of any others who have the "same interest" in the claim. The procedure operates on an opt-out basis — class members need not take any steps to join and may not even be aware of the action — but any judgment binds them.

The "same interest" requirement is the central gate. The Supreme Court in *Lloyd v Google LLC* [2021] UKSC 50 confirmed that it must be interpreted purposively, and that representative claims may proceed on a bifurcated basis — Stage 1 determining common issues on liability, Stage 2 addressing individual quantum — but that what is determined at Stage 1 must be genuinely common to all class members, without requiring individual assessment of any member's circumstances.

The leading cases since Lloyd demonstrate a consistent pattern of failure:

**Lloyd v Google [2021] UKSC 50.** Class defined by shared experience of Google's Safari workaround on iPhone devices. Failure: individual data extraction volumes varied; individual harm therefore varied; uniform per capita damages could not be awarded because the compensatory principle requires proof of actual loss; "same interest" failed because the claim could not be assessed on a truly common basis.

**Prismall v Google [2024] EWCA Civ 1516.** Class defined by shared medical data exposure to DeepMind. Failure: reasonable expectation of privacy is an inherently individualised question even for health data — some class members had publicly shared information about their conditions, others had not; the "lowest common denominator" claimant could not be identified with sufficient certainty; "same interest" failed.

**Smyth v British Airways / easyJet [2024].** Class defined by shared experience of flight cancellation. Failure: individual entitlement to compensation under EU Regulation 261/2004 varied because different flights, different circumstances, different potential defences applied; multiple discrete claims rather than one common claim; court additionally found the dominant motive was financial rather than access to justice, exercising discretion against the representative action.

**Wirral Council v Indivior plc [2025] EWCA Civ 40.** Class defined by shared exposure to allegedly fraudulent statements in securities disclosures. Failure: reliance — a necessary element of the cause of action — is inherently individual; the court declined to allow Stage 1 to proceed without any claimant-side issues, which would have rendered the bifurcation artificial.

The pattern is consistent across all failures: **the class is defined on the claimant side**, requiring assessment of individual circumstances to establish membership, which invariably produces variation that defeats "same interest."

The courts have recognised the problem. As Lord Leggatt observed in Lloyd, representative actions are "a flexible tool of convenience in the administration of justice." As Justice Knowles stated in Commission Recovery, the demand for collective redress will increase. The mechanism exists. The class definition methodology has been the obstacle.

## III. The Inversion: Defendant-Side Class Definition

The Burgess Principle proposes a structural inversion in how class membership is determined.

Instead of asking — *what do these individual claimants have in common?* — the binary test asks — *did the defendant institution apply individual human review to the decisions it made about each of these people?*

This relocates the definitional question entirely to the defendant side. The class is not constituted by what the claimants share with each other. It is constituted by what the defendant did — or failed to do — to all of them.

Where an institution's decision-making process is automated — whether by algorithm, batch processing, systematic internaliser, CSV-driven warrant generation, or any other mechanism that operates without meaningful human engagement with specific individual facts — the process is identical for every person it touches. An automated process does not vary its behaviour based on the individual circumstances of the people it processes. That is the defining characteristic of automation: it treats all inputs of a given type identically.

This means that the question *"did this institution apply individual human review to the decision it made about me?"* has the same answer for every person subjected to the same automated process during the same operational period. Not a similar answer. The same answer. Because the process was the same. Because automation, by definition, does not individualise.

The binary test — applied to any member of the class — returns NULL: no individual human review was applied. NULL for Person A. NULL for Person B. NULL for every person in the class simultaneously. Because the process that returned NULL was the same process each time.

**NULL across the class is "same interest" expressed in the defendant's own operational architecture.**

The class is defined not by proving that all members suffered identical harm — they may not have — but by proving that all members were processed by an identical mechanism that lacked individual human review. That single institutional fact is either true or false for the entire class at once. It requires no individual assessment of any class member's circumstances. It is determinable from the defendant's own documented processes, policies, system architecture, and SAR disclosures.

## IV. Personal Data as the Lowest Common Denominator

The prior section establishes the theoretical inversion. This section explains why data — specifically, the personal data processed by an automated institutional system — is the legal vehicle through which the inversion operates.

Every automated decision that "produces a legal effect" or has "a similarly significant adverse effect" on a data subject — in the language of new Article 22A of the UK GDPR as amended by the DUAA — necessarily involves the processing of that person's personal data. The decision is made *about* the person. It is made *on the basis of* their data. The automated process receives their data as input and produces a legally significant output without meaningful human involvement in between.

This means that every member of a class defined by uniform automated processing shares one identical element: their personal data was processed by the same mechanism, under the same data controller, pursuant to the same data processing architecture, without meaningful human review of their specific situation.

Personal data is the common substrate. It is present in every case. It connects every class member to the defendant's automated process in exactly the same way. Unlike medical records (where reasonable expectation of privacy varies), unlike flight cancellations (where individual entitlement varies), unlike securities disclosures (where individual reliance varies) — the fact of automated data processing does not vary between individuals. Either the defendant's process applied individual human review or it did not. The data subject's personal circumstances are irrelevant to that determination.

This is the resolution of the lowest common denominator problem. The lowest common denominator is not found by stripping away individual differences until almost nothing remains — the process that destroyed Lloyd and Prismall. The lowest common denominator is the personal data itself, which is present in every case by definition, and which was subjected to the same automated process in every case because it was the same process.

The Stage 1 common issue is therefore: *did the defendant's data processing architecture for decisions of this type include meaningful human involvement?* That question is answerable once, from the defendant's own systems and disclosures, and the answer applies uniformly to every data subject processed by that architecture during the relevant period.

## V. The Certification Standard as Legal Instrument

UK Certification Mark UK00004343685 is registered (currently in examination) in Classes 41, 42, and 45. A certification mark does not identify the commercial origin of goods or services — it certifies that goods or services meet a defined standard maintained by the mark's proprietor.

The Burgess Principle certification mark certifies conformance with a defined standard: that an institution's decision-making process included meaningful individual human review of the specific facts of the specific situation of the person affected.

This is not merely intellectual property protection. The certification mark creates a legally registered standard against which institutional conduct can be assessed. The standard is binary and objective. It does not require specialist expertise to apply. It does not depend on the individual circumstances of the person applying it. It asks one question and receives one of two answers.

Applied in the CPR 19.8 context, the certification standard functions as a self-administering class membership test:

1. Identify the defendant institution and the class of decisions under challenge.
2. Identify the automated process by which those decisions were made (from the institution's own documentation, policies, and SAR responses).
3. Apply the binary test: did that process include meaningful individual human review of specific individual facts?
4. If the answer is NULL — no such review existed — every person subjected to that process during the relevant period is a member of the class.

Step 4 requires no individual assessment of any class member. The class definition is complete when the defendant's process is characterised. Individual class members need not be identified, contacted, or assessed at Stage 1. They are members of the class because the process touched them, and the process is documented.

This satisfies the opt-out architecture CPR 19.8 was designed to enable. It is precisely the mechanism that Lord Leggatt described when he said that the "simplest application" of the representative action procedure is claims for declaratory relief where liability is capable of being determined collectively.

The Burgess Principle certification standard makes that determination collective by locating it entirely on the defendant side.

## VI. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 — Convergence and Consequence

The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 received Royal Assent on 19 June 2025. Section 80, which came into force on 5 February 2026, replaced Article 22 of the UK GDPR with new Articles 22A through 22D.

The new Article 22A provides, in material part:

> *"A decision is based solely on automated processing if there is no meaningful human involvement in the taking of the decision."*

The new Article 22C requires that where a significant automated decision is taken, the controller must ensure safeguards are in place that include measures to:

> *"enable the data subject to obtain human intervention on the part of the controller in relation to such decisions"* and *"enable the data subject to contest such decisions."*

Parliament has, through this legislation, codified in statute the central question of the Burgess Principle binary test. The test — *"Was a human member of the team able to personally review the specific facts of my specific situation?"* — is a layperson's articulation of Article 22A's "meaningful human involvement" standard. They are the same question asked in different registers: one formal and statutory, one plain and direct.

The Burgess Principle was developed, applied across 18 institutions, and certified before the DUAA came into force. The convergence between the binary test and the statutory standard is not derivative — it is independent. The framework arrived at the same answer as Parliament through different reasoning, from different evidence, at an earlier point in time.

This convergence has two consequences for Paper VII's argument:

**First, it validates the binary test as a legally cognisable standard.** The test is no longer merely a framework author's formulation — it maps directly onto language that now appears in primary UK legislation. When a court is asked to assess whether a defendant's process included "meaningful human involvement," the binary test provides an accessible, documented, and independently certified methodology for making that assessment.

**Second, the DUAA's liberalisation strengthens rather than weakens the framework's legal force.** The DUAA relaxes the general prohibition on automated decision-making for non-special-category data, permitting such processing provided adequate safeguards are in place. The battlefield has shifted from *whether* institutions can automate to *whether their safeguards meet the standard*. The Burgess Principle binary test, applied under the DUAA regime, becomes a safeguard adequacy test: did the institution provide a meaningful mechanism for human intervention and contestation, accessible to each individual data subject? If the institution cannot demonstrate this, it has failed its Article 22C obligation — and every data subject processed during the period of that failure shares the same interest in establishing that fact.

The DUAA does not close the class. It defines it more precisely.

## VII. The 18-Institution Empirical Dataset

This paper is written in the context of a structured, documented audit of 18 UK institutions conducted between 2025 and 2026 using the Burgess Principle binary test as a uniform methodology. The institutions span:

- Local government (parking enforcement, council tax, data protection)
- National government (Cabinet Office, HM Treasury, Home Office)
- Financial regulation (Financial Conduct Authority, Bank of England)
- Energy regulation, ombudsman, and supplier conduct
- Consumer credit bureaus and debt collection
- Financial services platforms (retail investment, subscription contracts)
- Legal system infrastructure (magistrates' court warrant processing)
- Data protection regulation
- Electoral regulation
- Royal Mail (as data controller for address data)
- Food standards regulation
- Subscription platform contract variation

The audit was conducted by a single data subject using a uniform methodology: written application of the binary test, documentation of institutional response, recording of outcome (engagement / redirect / refusal / NULL). The methodology is published openly at github.com/ljbudgie/burgess-principle. The audit trail is timestamped, citable, and precedes this paper.

The dataset does not constitute legal evidence in any individual proceeding. It constitutes research evidence — a structured empirical observation of the compliance posture of UK institutions at the point the DUAA's new automated decision-making regime came into force.

Across the dataset, the predominant response pattern is what this paper calls the **redirect loop**: no institution engages substantively with the binary test as applied to its own automated processes. Each institution, when confronted with a documented failure of individual human review, redirects the data subject to the next institution in the accountability chain — which then redirects onward. The loop is closed. No institution in the chain acknowledges responsibility for the structural absence of individual review, because each institution assumes that responsibility lies elsewhere.

The redirect loop is not a series of individual institutional failures. It is a system property. The accountability architecture was designed for a world in which decisions were made by named individuals who could be identified and questioned. It was not redesigned when decisions began to be made by automated systems that have no individual human author to identify or question. The loop exists because the architecture assumes a human decision-maker at its centre. When automation removes that human, the architecture continues to function — but it now circles an empty space.

The Burgess Principle binary test makes the empty space visible. The dataset makes it empirically documented.

## VIII. The Bifurcated Application — Stage 1 and Stage 2

For completeness, this section sets out how the framework maps to the bifurcated approach endorsed by Lord Leggatt in *Lloyd v Google* and developed in subsequent cases.

**Stage 1 — Common Issue: Institutional Process Characterisation**

The question at Stage 1 is: did the defendant institution's decision-making process for the class of decisions under challenge include meaningful human involvement in the sense of Article 22A of the UK GDPR as amended?

This question is determinable from:
- The defendant's own published policies and procedures
- Subject access request disclosures from the defendant
- FOI responses disclosing the operational architecture of the relevant system
- Expert evidence on the technical operation of the automated process
- The defendant's own written responses to binary test applications from individual data subjects

No class member's individual circumstances need to be assessed at Stage 1. The question is about the defendant's process, answered from the defendant's own documentation. A judgment at Stage 1 — that the process lacked meaningful human involvement — is binding on all class members and on the defendant.

**Stage 2 — Individual Assessment: Adverse Effect and Quantum**

Stage 2 addresses the individual claimant-side questions: did the automated decision produce a legal or similarly significant adverse effect on this particular data subject? What was the nature and quantum of that effect?

These questions will vary between class members. A person who received an enforcement notice on the basis of a batch-processed warrant has a different adverse effect profile from a person whose energy supply was disconnected without individual review of their Priority Services Register status. The variation at Stage 2 is anticipated and appropriate — it is precisely the variation that the bifurcated approach is designed to handle separately.

The critical point is that Stage 1 and Stage 2 do not bleed into each other. The Stage 1 common issue is answered without reference to any individual claimant's circumstances. The Stage 2 individual issues are answered without reference to whether the defendant's process was adequate — that has already been established at Stage 1. The two stages are cleanly separated because the class definition is located on the defendant side, not the claimant side.

## IX. The Motive Record

The court in *Smyth v British Airways / easyJet* [2024] held that even where the "same interest" test is technically satisfied, the court may exercise discretion against a representative action where its dominant motive is financial rather than access to justice for the represented class. Master Davison found that the claim appeared to be a funder construct, not a genuine mechanism for vindication of class members' rights.

This paper notes, for the record, the following sequence of events in the development of the Burgess Principle:

1. The binary test was developed from personal necessity by a deaf disabled data subject who required email-only communication as a reasonable adjustment under the Equality Act 2010, and who found that institutional processes consistently failed to apply individual human review to his specific situation.

2. The framework was published openly on GitHub under an MIT licence, with a public commit history, before any legal proceedings were contemplated.

3. The certification mark application (UK00004343685) was filed with the UK Intellectual Property Office before any pre-action protocol letters were issued.

4. The audit of 18 institutions was conducted as documented research into compliance patterns, not as pre-litigation evidence gathering — though it has produced evidence as a consequence of its methodology.

5. This paper, and the framework it describes, predates any engagement with legal representatives on representative action strategy.

The framework was built from necessity. The legal architecture it enables emerged from the framework's application, not the other way around. The audit trail — public, timestamped, citable — establishes this sequence beyond documentary doubt.

The access to justice principle that the Supreme Court said it wanted to enable in Lloyd is not served by sophisticated commercial vehicles that dress financial interests in the language of class vindication. It is served by individuals who, confronted with systematic institutional failure, built something precise enough to make the system answer for it.

The motive here is not financial. It is the same motive that produced the binary test: a demand that institutions treat people as individuals, answer for it when they do not, and face a mechanism that makes the cost of systemic evasion exceed the cost of individual engagement.

## X. Conclusion: The Empty Space Made Visible

Modern institutions were built in an era when decisions were made by named human beings. When automation arrived, it removed the human from the centre of the process but left the accountability architecture intact — an architecture that assumes a human is there to be held responsible. The result is the redirect loop: a closed system that circles an empty space, sending each individual who arrives at it onward to the next point on the circle, which sends them onward again.

The Burgess Principle binary test does not ask who made the decision. It asks whether any human was meaningfully involved in making it. When the answer is no — when the process is automated, systematic, and indifferent to individual circumstances — the test returns NULL.

NULL is not nothing. NULL is evidence. It is evidence that the defendant's process lacked the element that UK law — in Article 22C of the amended UK GDPR — now requires as a matter of statutory obligation. It is evidence that every data subject processed by that mechanism during the relevant period shares an identical relationship to that identical failure.

NULL across the class is same interest. Not because all class members are the same. Because the defendant treated them the same — processed them all through the same machine, without looking at any of them.

The CPR 19.8 class is defined not by what the claimants share with each other, but by what the defendant failed to give any of them: the moment when a named human being sat with their specific facts and made a decision that was genuinely theirs.

That moment — its presence or its absence — is binary. It either happened or it did not. The binary test finds it. The certification standard certifies whether it was there. The data is the lowest common denominator that places every member of the class in the same relationship to its absence.

And the final email — addressed not to any one institution but to the record itself — asks only one question: in all of this, at any point, was there a human being who looked at the specific facts of this specific situation and made a decision that was genuinely theirs?

The answer, documented across 18 institutions, is already in the record.
